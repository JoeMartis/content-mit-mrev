Illustrative Example: Two Particles

Calculate the center of mass of a system consisting of two particles.
Version 1: Unequal masses. [show]
Consider a 2 particle system of masses m1 and m2 , with m1 = 2 m2 . The masses are separated a distance d.

a) What is the position of the center of mass of the system with respect to a coordinate system which origin is located at particle 1 and particle 2 is in the +x-axis.

Answer: [show]
\(\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{1}{3}d\;\hat{i}\)
Derivation: [show]

In the coordinate system with origin in particle 1 (see figure above), the positions of particles 1 and 2 are written as:

\(\vec{r}_1=0\)

\(\vec{r}_2=d\hat{i}\)

Then, the position of the center of mass is:

\(\vec{R}_{cm}=\frac{m_1\vec{r}_1+m_2\vec{r}_2}{m_1+m_2}=\)

\(\vec{R}_{cm}=\frac{m_10+m_2d\;\hat{i}}{m_1+m_2}=\)

\(\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{m_2d}{2m_2+m_2}\hat{i}=\frac{1}{3}d\;\hat{i}\)

The center of mass, red cross in the figure, is located along the line joining both masses at a distance d/3 from particle 1, the more massive particle.

b) What will be your answer if the origin of the coordinate system is in particle 2?

Answer: [show]
\(\vec{R}_{cm} = -\frac{2}{3}d\;\hat{i}\)
Derivation: [show]

The position vectors of the two particles measured from the new coordinate system:

\(\vec{r}_1=-d\hat{i}\)

\(\vec{r}_2=0\)

and the position of the center of mass:

\(\vec{R}_{cm}=\frac{m_1\vec{r}_1+m_2\vec{r}_2}{m_1+m_2}=\)

\(\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{m_1(-d)}{m_1+m_2}\hat{i}=\)

\(\vec{R}_{cm}=-\frac{2m_2d}{2m_2+m_2}=-\frac{2}{3}d\;\hat{i}\)


Comparing the above result, with the answer obtained in part a):

\(\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{1}{3}d\;\hat{i}\)

we conclude that the position of the center of mass depends on the coordinate system used but its distance relative to particle 1 is still d/3

Version 2: Equal masses. [show]
Consider a 2 particle system with equal masses, M1 = M2. Calculate the position of the center of mass in meters (m) if:

The position of particle 1:

\(\vec{r}_1=3\mbox{m }\hat{j}\)

The position of particle 2:

\(\vec{r_2}= 6\mbox{m }\hat{i}+5\mbox{m }\hat{j}\)

Answer: [show]
\(\vec{R}_{cm} = 3\mbox{m}\hat{i}+4\mbox{m}\hat{j}\)
Derivation: [show]
Knowing the position vectors of the two particles

\(\vec{r}_1=3\mbox{m}\hat{j}\)

\(\vec{r_2}= 6\mbox{m}\hat{i}+5\mbox{m}\hat{j}\),

then, the position of the center of mass is:

\(\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{M_1\vec{r}_1+M_2\vec{r}_2}{M_1+M_2}=\)

\(\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{M_13\mbox{m}\hat{j}+M_2(6\mbox{m}\hat{i}+5\mbox{m}\hat{j})}{M_1+M_2}=\)

Knowing that M1 = M2, the masses cancel to have:

\(\vec{R}_{cm} = \frac{6\mbox{m}\hat{i}+8\mbox{m}\hat{j}}{2}=\)

\(\vec{R}_{cm} = 3\mbox{m}\hat{i}+4\mbox{m}\hat{j}\)


The center of mass is shown in red in the figure. It is located along the line joining both masses and at the midpoint between the equal masses.

A better choice of coordinate system for this problem is one with either the x- or y- axis passing through both particles.
