Momentum and Impulse Model

Learning Goals

After completing this module you should be able to:

  • Define momentum
  • Define impulse in terms of force
  • Define impulse in terms of momentum.

Allowable Systems

This model is applicable to systems consisting of multiple point particles (or centers of mass of rigid bodies).

Cues

The model is especially useful when describing the momentum of systems where external forces are absent (system momentum will be constant) or estimating the external force in a process that occurs in a very short time interval such as collisions or explosions (where impulse can be determined from change of momentum).

Relevant Definitions

Momentum

Newton's Principia DEFINITION II [Cajori translation]: “The quantity of motion is the measure of the same, arising from the velocity, and the quantity of matter, jointly.”

We call this quantity momentum, which is traditionally given the symbol p (partly for "impulse", and since m is already taken):

\( \vec{p} = m\vec{v} \)

for a single object of mass m traveling with velocity v. 

For a system composed of multiple masses, this becomes:

\(\vec{p}^{system} = \sum_i m_i\vec{v}_i\)

Impulse

Impulse (which is given the symbol J) is defined by:

\(\vec{J}_{fi} = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} \vec{F}^{ext}\;dt\)

for a single force acting over a time interval ti to tf.

Relevant Interactions

The agent of change for momentum is external force, meaning forces acting on objects in the system due to their interactions with objects outside the system. The key point about momentum is that internal forces (no matter how large) do not change a system's total momentum.

Laws of Change

Integral Form — especially useful when momentum changes little

\(\vec{p}_{f} = \vec{p}_{i}+\sum \vec{J}^{ext}_{fi} = \vec{p}_{i}+\int_{t_i}^{t_f} \sum \vec{F}^{ext}\;dt\)

Differential Form

\(\frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = \sum \vec{F}^{ext}\)

Vector Nature

It is important to note that momentum is a vector. The direction of the momentum of an object is in the direction of the object's velocity. For a system of objects, the momentum is in the direction of the velocity of the system's center of mass.

Dimensions and Units

The dimensions of momentum, by its definition, are [mass] * [length]/[time]. In SI units momentum is measured in kg m/s.
