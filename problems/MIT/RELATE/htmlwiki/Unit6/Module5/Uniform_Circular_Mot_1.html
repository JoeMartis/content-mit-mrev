Uniform Circular Motion

So far we have only discussed models involving one dimension of motion (e.g. constant acceleration, constant velocity). In this module, we introduce a special case of two-dimensional motion: motion in a circle of constant radius at constant speed, which is referred to as uniform circular motion. The "uniform" refers to constant speed, the "circular" refers to having constant radius from some center. Every other variable - position, velocity, and acceleration - oscillate in time at the angular frequency of rotation, generally represented by \(\omega\).

In using previous models, we specified the net force and acceleration experienced by an object, then integrated the acceleration to find the velocity and position. For uniform circular motion, we specify the motion in terms of position and velocity first, then differentiate to find the acceleration. The acceleration is radially inward and is given a special name - centripetal acceleration.

Warning: This special name for acceleration often leads to the incorrect idea that there is a new kind of force called "centripetal force". Yes, there must be an inward (center-directed) force to make the object move in a circle, but it is simply the familiar "net force" in Newton's Second Law - the vector sum of forces like string tension, gravity, contact forces, etc.

Learning Goals

After working through this module, you should be able to:

  • Explain how it is possible that an object moving with constant speed can be undergoing an acceleration.
  • Define centripetal acceleration.
  • Draw free body diagrams for objects executing uniform circular motion.
