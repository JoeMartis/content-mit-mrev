Changing Interactions and Constraints

Motions observed in the real world frequently involve changing interactions. The models introduced in this course, however, often assume constant interactions. Fortunately, complex motions can often be decomposed into a sequence of motions with constant interactions. For example, a spring gun has harmonic motion when the projectile is pushed by the spring and free fall projectile motion thereafter.

Learning Goals

After working through this module, you should be able to:

  • Analyze complex motions by decomposing them into different temporal or spatially separated segments during which the motion is well-approximated by a standard model.
  • Assemble a continuous description of a motion by applying constraints ("boundary conditions") to the position and velocity at the transitions from one model to the next.

Decomposing Motion Using Multiple Sequential Models

The general procedure for dealing with such a case is summarized below.

Steps for Decomposing a Problem Involving Changing Interactions
  1. Split the motion into time intervals during which one or another model applies.
  2. Determine a general description of the motion during each interval.
  3. Reassemble the complete motion by matching the final conditions of each interval to the initial conditions of the next interval.

When decomposing motion into different segments, each of which involves a different model, it is necessary to use a general "initial value" formulation of the model. Errors often arise from using a form of the equations that assumes ti = 0, xi = 0 or vi = 0 for initial values.

The key step in the procedure given above is the notion of applying the constraint that the final conditions of one interval will become the initial conditions of the next interval. Often this simply requires that the two models agree on the position and velocity of the object at the boundary where the applicable model changes. We have already approached a problem in this way: the example of a student walking around a college campus used in the Describing Motion unit.
