Utility of Multiple Simultaneous Models

Describing the motion of a single system sometimes necessitates the simultaneous application of multiple models. An example of this is the motion of an object released into freefall with a nonzero horizontal component to its initial velocity. Such an object is called a projectile and its motion is called projectile motion.

Learning Goals

After working through this module you should be able to:

  • Explain the significance of initial conditions in distinguishing between freefall and projectile motion.
  • Explain why two Models in the hierarchy are needed to describe projectile motion.
  • Use time as a constraint to couple the equations governing the horizontal and vertical motion of a projectile.

Projectile Motion

Like any object in freefall near the Earth, projectiles experience the constant acceleration of gravity, 9.8 m/s2 directed downward (assuming air resistance is negligible). Unlike the motion of an object thrown directly upward or directly downward, however, projectile motion is inherently two-dimensional. Projectiles do not follow a straight line as they move. Instead, as illustrated in the applet below, projectiles trace out a segment of a parabola as they move. Thus, it might seem that we need a new model (something like "two-dimensional motion with constant acceleration") to represent the motion of a projectile. By decomposing vectors into components, however, we can actually employ the models we have already developed to describe projectile motion.

Note that gravitational acceleration is purely vertical. The x-component of the projectile's motion experiences no acceleration while the y-component experiences freefall. Thus, the two-dimensional motion of a projectile can be described by saying that the projectile is governed simultaneously by two one-dimensional models: the x-component motion is governed by the Constant Velocity (Zero Acceleration) model and the y-component motion is governed by the One-Dimensional Motion with Constant Acceleration model.

Constraints on Multiple Models

When multiple models apply to a single system, it is vitally important to recognize what physical variables appear in both of the models. These quantities will serve to constrain the equations and allow us to solve them. In projectile motion, the quantity that serves to tie the x and y directions together is time. Unless you really enjoy nasty algebra, it is strongly recommended that you set up your equations such that the time coordinate is the same in the x-motion and the y-motion. The advantage of making this choice is clear: if you can find the time associated with an important event by looking at the y-motion (for example the time to reach maximum height or the time to hit the ground) you can use that value to determine the x-coordinate at the same instant, or vice versa.
