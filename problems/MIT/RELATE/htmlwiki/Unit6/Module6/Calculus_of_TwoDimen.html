Generic Two-Dimensional Motion

In the module on "Uniform Circular Motion" we saw that motion in a circle requires a net force inwards (centripetal), even when speed is constant. Velocity, a vector, is not constant, because the direction of motion changes. Acceleration due to curvilinear motion is a more difficult concept for many to grasp than straight-line motion with changing speed. Understanding that velocity in general can change because of either a change in speed or a change in direction (or both, of course) is the point of this module. We give a general discussion of position, velocity, and acceleration vectors in two dimensions.

Learning Goals

  • To be able to visualize the derivative of a vector and the procedure for finding it.
  • To be able to express the acceleration of an object moving in a circle of radius R in terms of either its linear or angular velocity.

The Derivative of a Vector, e.g. Velocity

You should know that the derivative of a function of a single variable is defined as the change in that function over a short interval in the limit where that interval goes to zero. For example if the position of a particle moving in 1-Dimension is x(t), the velocity is:

\(v_x = \frac{dx}{dt} {=} ^{lim}_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{x(t+\Delta t)-x(t)}{\Delta t}\)

The expression, the ratio of the change in x to the time interval during which the change occurs, is the rate of change - how rapidly the function x(t) changes when t changes. Rate of change is the central concept of the derivative.

The derivative of a vector quantity is a mathematical generalization of this idea. For a position vector \(\vec r(t)\), the definition (vector) velocity is:

\(\vec{v}(t)=\frac{d\vec{r}}{dt} {=}^{lim}_{\Delta t\to 0}\frac{\vec r(t+\Delta t)-\vec r(t)}{\Delta t}\)

Several things are worth noting:

  • The result is a vector
  • The result does not depend on the location of the origin of the coordinate system. Changing the position of the origin would change both \(\vec r(t)\) and \(\vec r(t+\Delta t)\) by the same amount and thus would leave \(\Delta \vec r\) unchanged.
