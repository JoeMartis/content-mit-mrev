Introduction to Constant Acceleration with Constant Net Force

This model applies to a single direction of a particle's motion when the net force, and hence the acceleration, along that direction is constant. It is a one dimensional model since we can choose a coordinate axis which aligns with the direction of motion. Typically this might be a system subject to gravity (along the y-axis) or one that is physically constrained to move in only one direction (e.g. on a straight track). The driving force along this coordinate must be constant in time. This model is a  special case of the General One-Dimensional Motion model with the added constraint a(t)=constant (i.e. da/dt = 0 ).

Note: an analogous model applies to rotational motion about an axis under the influence of a constant torque.

Learning Goals

  • Recognize when a motion diagram, graph, equation or table of data represents an object moving with constant acceleration.
  • Describe the motion of the object represented in these ways.
  • Predict the position and velocity at an arbitrary later time given initial velocity and position.
  • Know and apply the time-independent form that relates the initial and final velocity as functions of position (not time) for motion with constant acceleration.

Model: Constant Acceleration with Constant Net Force

Applicable Systems

Either a single point particle or the center of mass of a rigid body (or rotating body) that is subject to a constant net applied force (or torque), even if this is only the case along one coordinate axis.

Relevant Interactions

Constant net force must be present to cause motion with a constant acceleration along that one coordinate.

Laws of Change

For motion with constant acceleration a.

Integral Form

\( v(t) = v_{i,x}+ a t\)

\( x(t) = x_{i}+v_{i,x}t+ \frac{1}{2}a_xt^{2}\)

NOTE: In the above expressions, The initial time - the time when the position and velocity equal \(x_i\) and \(v_{i,x}\) - has been taken to be t=0. Often the initial conditions are given at a later time ti, for example if a rocket is fired at ti  at a target whose motion started at t=0. To accommodate constant acceleration with initial conditions given at ti, these equations may be generalized to:

\( v(t) = v_{i,x}+ a_x (t - t_{i})\)

\( x(t) = x_{i}+v_{i,x}(t-t_{i})+ \frac{1}{2}a_x(t-t_{i})^{2}\)

Differential Form

\(\frac{dv_x}{dt}=a_x\)

Form Without Dependence on Time

\(v^{2}(x)= v_{i,x}^{2}+ 2 a_x (x - x_{i})\)

This is a useful expression, because time has been eliminated as a variable. Thus velocity can be thought of as a function of position. Foreshadowing note: this formula leads the way to kinetic energy and work (multiply by 1/2 m and substitute F for ma).

Average Velocity Form

For constant acceleration, the average velocity is exactly the average of the initial and final velocities. Therefore, the position can be expressed in terms of these variables as:

\(x(t) = x_{i}+\frac{1}{2}(v_{f,x}+v_{i,x})(t - t_{i})\)
