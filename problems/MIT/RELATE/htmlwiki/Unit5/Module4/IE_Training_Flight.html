Illustrative Example: Training Flight

Note: this example makes use of the System-Interaction-Model (SIM) approach which will be explained in the next unit. If you have not read about SIM yet, do not worry too much about the formal notation for now. The words "system", "interactions" and "model" are used in the same way we have been using them throughout.

Photo by Craig Kinzer courtesy wikimedia commons

The Piper Tomahawk (shown above) is widely used for flying lessons. It has a liftoff speed of about 55 knots and a landing speed of about 46 knots. The listed ground-roll for takeoff is 820 feet, and for landing is 635 feet.

Part A: Assuming constant acceleration, what is the Tomahawk's acceleration during the takeoff run? [solution]
System: [show system]
We will treat the Tomahawk as a point particle.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The assumption of constant acceleration required in the problem statement suggests that the thrust of the air on the plane's propeller, the air resistance on the plane, the rolling friction of the wheels, and any other influences add to give a constant forward net force, and hence a constant forward acceleration.

Model: [show Model]
"One-Dimensional Motion with Constant Acceleration"

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

Once we have determined that we are using the 1-D Motion with Constant Acceleration Model, we have essentially reduced the problem to math. The Constant Acceleration Model is somewhat unique, however, in the fact that there are a large number of equations to choose from when applying the Model. In order to select the appropriate equation, we have to clearly understand the information presented in the problem (called the givens) and also what we are trying to find (the unknowns).

Step 1: Develop a Coordinate System [show]
To understand the givens and unknowns, it is a good idea to first develop a coordinate system. By drawing out the runway and marking where the plane starts its takeoff run (x = 0 in the picture) and where it lifts off the ground (820 feet in the picture) we can see that the 820 feet given in the problem statement is a distance not a position. The difference between the plane's liftoff position and the starting point is 820 feet. Thus, in terms of equations, we have both the final value of x (820 feet in our coordinates) and the initial value of x (0 feet in our coordinates).

Step 2: Summarize the Givens [show]
In this part we are focused only on the takeoff run. Thus, the initial speed is assumed to be 0 (takeoff from rest) and the final speed is 55 knots. We ignore the 46 knots and the 635 feet, as these describe the landing roll, not the takeoff run. Thus, for the takeoff run, we have:
Part A Givens
\( x_{i} = 0 \mbox{ feet} \qquad x_{f} = 820 \mbox{ feet} \qquad v_{i,x} = 0 \mbox{ knots} \qquad v_{f,x} = 55 \mbox{ knots}\)

Before proceeding, however, we should really convert these givens to a consistent set of units. It is always safest when solving a physics problem to convert to SI units. The appropriate conversions in this case are: 1 foot = 3.281 m and 1 knot = 0.514 m/s. After conversions, the givens are:

Converted Part A Givens
\( x_{i} = 0 \mbox{ m} \qquad x_{f} = 250 \mbox{ m} \qquad v_{i,x} = 0 \mbox{ m/s} \qquad v_{f,x} = 28.3 \mbox{ m/s}\)

Note that we have retained three significant figures for the final velocity (and also for the position, though it is not obvious) even though the original given, 55 knots, contained only 2 significant figures. This is not a mistake. When performing conversions, you should treat them as intermediate steps, not final answers. Retain extra digits through all intermediate steps and round appropriately only at the end.
Step 3: Determine the Unknown [show]
We are asked to find the plane's acceleration during the takeoff run, thus, the unknown for this problem is ax.
Step 4: Select an Equation [show]
The equation which is a representation of our chosen Model that involves all the givens and also the unknown for Part A is:

\(v_{f,x}^{2} = v_{i,x}^{2} + 2a_{x}(x_{f}-x_{i}) \)
Step 5: Symbolic Algebra [show]
Now that we have the givens, unknown and equation, we rearrange algebraically to isolate the unknown. It is good practice to solve algebraically with variables before substituting the numbers. The only exception is that you are encouraged to substitute any zeros before doing the algebra. Substituting zeros will greatly simplify the expression in many cases. In this case, because the initial position and velocity are each zero, we can write:

\(v_{f,x}^{2} = 2a_{x}x_{f}\) and solving for the acceleration gives: \(a_{x} = \frac{v_{f,x}^{2}}{2x_{f}}\)
Step 6: Make Sense of the Expression [show]
At this stage, it is good to check your algebra by considering some simple limits. Look at the expression found for the acceleration. If the final velocity is zero, then the acceleration will also be zero. Does this make sense? If the final position goes to zero, the acceleration approaches infinity. Does this make sense?
Step 7: Substitute Numbers [show]
Only after essentially finishing the problem should numbers be substituted. In our case, we find:

\( a = \frac{(\mbox{28.3 m/s})^{2}}{2(\mbox{250 m})} = \mbox{1.6 m/s}^{2}\)

Remember that, like velocity and position, acceleration is a vector. Thus, technically we should report a direction with our numerical answer. In this case, reporting a direction is tricky since we do not know which way the plane is moving. The best we can do is to say that the answer is 1.6 m/s2 in the direction of the plane's motion. Note also that when substituting numbers the units behave like variables: we have canceled one factor of "meters" in the numerator with the "meters" in the denominator, leaving units of meters per second squared. This cancellation highlights the importance of converting to a consistent set of units. Imagine if we had left our quantities in units of knots and feet. We would have ended with knots squared per foot -- a very unwieldy unit!
Step 8: Estimation of Numerical Value [show]
Now that we have the numerical answer, it pays to check the size of the answer against your intuition. Right now, you probably have very little intuition about these numbers, but as you work through the course you will develop some. A good baseline for acceleration values is to compare them with the gravitational acceleration of 9.8 m/s2. "Safe" accelerations should be less than this value, but not too much less or the acceleration is unnoticeable. A value of 1.6 m/s2 is reasonable for a plane: large enough to be noticeable, but smaller than the gravitational acceleration.
Part B: Again assuming constant acceleration, what is the elapsed time from the instant the Tomahawk begins its takeoff run until the instant it leaves the ground? [solution]
System, Interactions and Model: Same as Part A.
Quantitative Approach:[show approach]

At the conclusion of Part A, we have the givens:

Part B Givens
\(x_{i} = \mbox{0 m} \qquad x_{f} = \mbox{250 m} \qquad v_{i,x} = \mbox{0 m/s} \qquad v_{f,x} = \mbox{28.3 m/s}\qquad a_{x} = \mbox{1.6 m/s}^{2}\)

Note that we have included the acceleration found in Part A as a given for Part B. This extra given makes an enormous difference in the next step of the problem. Instead of one path to the solution, we can now find at least three different equations that are mathematical representations of the chosen Model and that will give us the answer we seek. This kind of redundancy is common in the 1-D Motion with Constant Acceleration Model. To illustrate, we will solve the problem using different methods.

For simplicity, we will choose our time coordinates such that ti = 0.

Method 1: Average velocity. [show]
Suppose you were unable to solve Part A. It is possible to solve Part B without knowing that the acceleration is 1.6 m/s2. The equation

\( x_{f} = x_{i} + \frac{1}{2}(v_{f,x}+v_{i,x})(t_{f} - t_{i})\)

becomes, after using the zeros:

\( x_{f} = \frac{1}{2}v_{f,x}t_{f}\)

which is solved to give:

\( t_{f} = \frac{2x_{f}}{v_{f,x}} = \mbox{18 s}\)
Method 2: Velocity as a function of time. [show]
If you did solve Part A first, you can use the simplest of the mathematical representations of the Model to obtain an answer to Part B:

vx(t) = vi,x + ax(tf − ti)

After using the zeros and substituting t = tf, we have:

vf,x = axtf

which gives:

\( t_{f} = \frac{v_{f,x}}{a_{x}} = \mbox{18 s}\)
Method 3: Position as a function of time. [show]
A more complicated method that relies on knowing the acceleration is to use:

\( x(t) = x_{i} + v_{i,x}(t-t_{i}) + \frac{1}{2}a_{x}(t-t_{i})^{2}\)

After substituting the appropriate zeros and also t = tf, we have:

\( x_{f} = \frac{1}{2}a_{x}t_{f}^{2}\)

which is solved to give:

\( t_{f} = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2 x_{f}}{a_{x}}} = \pm \mbox{18 s} \)

We select the plus sign since the plane began its run at ti = 0. It cannot possibly take off before it began to move! In fact, our Model is not applicable (with the given acceleration) before t = 0, since the plane experiences a change in its interactions at that time.
Part C: How far does the plane travel in the first 9.0 seconds of the takeoff run? [solution]
System, Interactions and Model: Same as previous parts.
Quantitative Approach:[show approach]

Based upon the results of Part B, we know that the plane has not reached its takeoff speed within 9.0 seconds, and therefore it has also not traveled the full takeoff distance. Thus, we are left the givens:

Part C Givens
\(x_{i} = \mbox{0 m} \qquad v_{i,x}=\mbox{0 m/s} \qquad t_{i} = \mbox{0 s} \qquad \mbox{t = 9.0 s} \qquad a_{x} = \mbox{1.6 m/s}^{2}\)

The unknown that we seek is x(t=9.0 s), the location at t = 9.0 s. The most direct approach is to use:

\(x(t) = x_{i} + v_{i,x}(t-t_{i}) + \frac{1}{2}a_{x}(t-t_{i})^{2}\)

No algebra is necessary, since x is already isolated. We simply substitute to find:

\(\mathcal{} x(\mbox{9.0 s}) = \mbox{65 m}\)
Part D: What is the acceleration (assumed constant) during landing? [solution]
System: [show system]
We will treat the Tomahawk as a point particle.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The assumption of constant acceleration required in the problem statement suggests that the thrust of the air on the plane's propeller, the air resistance on the plane, the rolling friction of the wheels, and any other influences add to give a constant backward net force, and hence a constant backward acceleration.

Model: [show Model]
"One-Dimensional Motion with Constant Acceleration"

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

This problem is essentially identical in structure to Part A, except that when landing the plane's initial speed is nonzero and its final speed is zero. Thus, we work the problem just as we did Part A. We first define a coordinate system

and then summarize the givens (don't forget to convert to SI units)

Converted Part D Givens
\(x_{i} = \mbox{0 m} \qquad x_{f} = \mbox{194 m} \qquad v_{i,x} = \mbox{23.6 m/s} \qquad v_{f,x} = \mbox{0 m/s}\)

Just as in Part A, the simplest equation to use is:

\( v_{f,x}^{2} = v_{i,x}^{2} + 2 a_{x} (x_{f} - x_{i})\)

After substituting zeros, this becomes:

\( 0 = v_{i,x}^{2} + 2a_{x} x_{f}\)

which can be rearranged to give:

\(a = \frac{-v_{i,x}^{2}}{2x_{f}} = -\mbox{1.4 m/s}^{2}\)

The magnitude of this acceleration is simply 1.4 m/s2 To report it as a vector, the best approach is probably to say the acceleration is 1.4 m/s2 in the direction opposite the plane's motion.
Part E: How far does the plane travel in the first 9.0 s after landing? [solution]
System, Interactions and Model: As in Part D.
Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

This problem is similar to Part C. Again, 9.0 seconds is not the full duration of the plane's acceleration, so we cannot assume the final speed is zero or the final position is 194 m. The givens that we do have, including the result of Part D, are:

Converted Part E Givens
\(x_{i} = \mbox{0 m} \qquad v_{i,x} = \mbox{23.6 m/s} \qquad a_{x} = -\mbox{1.4 m/s}^{2}\)

As in Part C, we will choose our time coordinates such that ti = 0, and we again employ the equation:

\( x(t) = x_{i} + v_{i,x}(t-t_{i}) + \frac{1}{2}a_{x}(t-t_{i})^{2}\)

after substituting zeros, this becomes:

\( x(t) = v_{i,x}t + \frac{1}{2}a_{x} t^{2}\)

Note that the initial velocity is not zero for a landing plane, and so cannot be canceled.

We can now substitute t= 9.0 s to find:

\( x = (\mbox{23.6 m/s})(\mbox{9.0 s}) + \frac{1}{2}(-\mbox{1.4 m/s}^{2})(\mbox{9.0 s})^{2} = \mbox{156 m}\)

Putting in the numbers shows the importance of the negative sign associated with the acceleration (we found the acceleration to be negative in Part D). If you were to input a positive acceleration, the plane would cover more distance in the 9.0 s than it would simply coasting at the initial landing speed!
