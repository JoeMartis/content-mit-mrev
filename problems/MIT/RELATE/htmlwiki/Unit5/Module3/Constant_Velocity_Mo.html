Model: Constant Velocity and Zero Net Force Model

Applicable Systems

Either a single point particle or the center of mass of a rigid body, provided the system is subject to zero net applied force.

Relevant Interactions

The net force must be zero. This does not imply the absence of any force, only that the vector sum of all forces acting on the particle must be zero. In other words any external forces, if present, must cancel each other out.

Laws of Change

For motion with constant velocity, the acceleration is \(a=0\).

Differential Form

\[\frac{dv_x}{dt}=0\]

Integral Form

The corresponding integral is rather trivial, \[v_x(t) = v_{i,x}= \text{constant}\]

Therefore it is customary to consider the position vs. time - the integral of the velocity - as the integral form of this model: \[x(t) = x_{i}+v_{i,x}t\]

NOTE: In the above expressions, the initial time - the time when the position and velocity equal \(x_i \) and \(v_{i,x}\) - has been taken to be \(t=0\). Often the initial conditions are given at an arbitrary time \(t_i\), for example if a bullet moving with constant velocity is fired at time \(t_i\) at a target whose motion started at \(t=0\). To accommodate constant velocity with initial conditions give at \(t_i \), this equation may be generalized to: \[x(t) = x_{i}+v_{i,x}(t-t_{i})\]

NOTE: An analogous relationship applies to the angular velocity when the net torque is zero (we take \(t_i=0 \) for simplicity): \[\omega(t) = \omega_{i}= \text{constant}\] which means that the orientation angle of the rigid body increases linearly with time, \[\theta(t) = \theta_{i}+\omega_{i}t\]
