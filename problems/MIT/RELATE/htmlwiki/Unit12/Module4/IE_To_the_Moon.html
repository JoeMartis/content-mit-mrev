Illustrative Example: To the Moon

Photo by Michael Collins courtesy of NASA

The NASA Apollo program ran 17 missions to the Moon between 1961 and 1972  (6 unmanned and 11 manned). Many of the orbital parameters of the Apollo missions are freely available online from NASA. In this module, we will use data from the Apollo missions and simultaneous application of multiple models to solve orbit problems.

Part A: The trajectory taken from Earth brought Apollo 11 to a height of about 160,000 m above the Moon's surface moving at approximately 2500 m/s. Show that without a course correction, Apollo 11 would have simply flown by the Moon and escaped its gravity. The mass of the Moon is 7.36x1022 kg and the radius of the moon is about 1,740,000 m. [show]
System: [show system]
The Apollo 11 craft will be approximated as a point particle and the Moon as an infinitely massive object.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The gravitational interaction between the spacecraft and the earth.

Models: [show Models]
Constant Mechanical Energy Model.

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

The Importance of Negative Energy [show]
The mechanical energy of an object in a bound orbit must be negative, as shown in the previous module. If the orbit has positive energy, the apoapsis distance will be infinite, meaning the orbiting object effectively escapes the gravitational pull of the central body.
Set Up the Mechanical Energy [show]
The equation for the mechanical energy of the orbit

\( E = \frac{1}{2}mv^{2} - \frac{GmM}{r}\)

where M is the mass of the Earth in this problem and m is the mass of the Apollo 11 spacecraft.
Why Don't We Need m? [show]
Our expression for energy involves the mass of the spacecraft, which was not given. We could certainly look this up, but we do not have to. We are only interested in determining if the energy is positive or negative. Since both terms are linear in m, we can divide it to the left side:

\( \frac{E}{m} = \frac{1}{2}v^{2} - \frac{GM}{r} \)

Then, since m is always positive, we know that the (plus or minus) sign of E will depend only on the quantity that remains on the right hand side of the above expression (which is completely determined). This is a reminder that the orbital trajectory does not depend on the mass of the orbiting object.
What is r? [show]

It is important to remember that the variable r is the distance measured from the center of the Moon, thus, for this example,

\( r = r_{\rm Moon} + r_{\rm above} = 1,740,000\;{\rm m} + 160,000\;{\rm m} = 1,900,000\;{\rm m} \)
Evaluate the Expression [show]

We are finally in a position to evaluate:

\( \frac{1}{2}v^{2} - \frac{GM}{r} = 0.5 \times (2500\;{\rm m/s})^{2} - \frac{(6.67\times 10^{-11}\;\frac{{\rm Nm}^{2}}{{\rm kg}^{2}}) \times (7.36\times 10^{22} \;{\rm kg})}{1,900,000\;{\rm m}} = 541,000\;\frac{{\rm m}^{2}}{{\rm s}^{2}}\)

which is greater than zero.
Understand the Answer [show]

The sign of the energy is positive, so the Apollo 11 spacecraft would have escaped the Moon's gravity without a corrective action.
Part B: The corrective action taken by the Apollo 11 crew was a 360 s burn of the engine to slow the spacecraft's speed to 1670 m/s which also caused the craft to descend toward the Moon. This brought the spacecraft into a bound elliptical orbit with a periapsis of about 110,000 m above the Moon's surface. What was the approximate apoapsis distance and speed of this orbit? [show]
System: [show system]
The Apollo 11 craft will be approximated as a point particle and the Moon as an infinitely massive object.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The gravitational interaction between the spacecraft and the earth.

Models: [show Models]
Keplerian Orbit Model, Constant Angular Momentum Model and Constant Mechanical Energy Model apply simultaneously.

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

Understand the Givens [show]
Both conservation of angular momentum and conservation of mechanical energy relate four orbital parameters (assuming the mass of the central object is known): the speed at periapsis, the speed at apoapsis, the periapsis distance and the apoapsis distance. Two of these are given (the speed and distance at periapsis). Since we have two unknowns, we will have to employ two independent equations in order to find a solution. Thus, we must simultaneously apply conservation of angular momentum and conservation of mechanical energy to solve.
Combine the Equations [show]
The equation for conservation of angular momentum in an elliptical Keplerian orbit can be written:

\( v_{a}r_{a} = v_{p}r_{p}\;\)

where the subscripts stand for apoapsis and periapsis.

The equation for conservation of mechanical energy can be written:

\( \frac{1}{2}v_{a}^{2} - \frac{GM}{r_{a}} = \frac{1}{2}v_{p}^{2} - \frac{GM}{r_{p}} \)

where M is the mass of the Earth in this problem.

We are asked to determine the apoapsis distance and speed. One way to work the algebra is to solve the angular momentum equation for the unknown speed at apoapsis:

\( v_{a} = \frac{v_{p}r_{p}}{r_{a}}\)

and then substitute this expression in to eliminate the apoapsis speed from the energy equation:

\( \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{v_{p}r_{p}}{r_{a}}\right)^{2} - \frac{GM}{r_{a}} = \frac{1}{2}v_{p}^{2} - \frac{GM}{r_{p}}\)

It is important to note that this resulting equation is quadratic in the desired quantity (ra).
Solve the Quadratic [show]
In order to solve, we must get the equation into standard quadratic form. To do this, we multiply both sides by ra2:

\( \frac{1}{2}v_{p}^{2}r_{p}^{2} - GMr_{a} = \left(\frac{1}{2}v_{p}^{2}-\frac{GM}{r_{p}}\right)r_{a}^{2} \)

and then getting all terms on one side:

\( \left(\frac{GM}{r_{p}} -\frac{1}{2}v_{p}^{2}\right)r_{a}^{2} - GMr_{a} + \frac{1}{2}v_{p}^{2}r_{p}^{2} = 0 \)

which implies that:
Don't Forget the Moon's Radius [show]
Before substituting into the solution for the quadratic, we must remember that, as we discussed in Part A, the proper periapsis distance to use in the formula is measured from the center of the moon. Thus, rp = 110,000 m + 1,740,000 m = 1,850,000 m and the quadratic equation gives:

\( r_{a} = 2,050,000 \;{\rm m}\qquad{\rm or}\qquad r_{a} = 1,850,000\;{\rm m} \)
Understand the Answer [show]
We have obtained two possible answers from the quadratic equation. One of them, however, is simply the periapsis distance again. It is important to remember that both the angular momentum conservation equation and the mechanical energy conservation equation can be satisfied by putting the periapsis distance and velocity on both sides. The other solution is the actual apoapsis distance. It is reasonable to wonder if the presence of the periapsis radius in the solution set implies that a circular orbit is possible. The fact that this is not possible is confirmed by solving Part C below (if the orbit was truly circular, the discriminant of the quadratic would have been zero and both solutions would have been equal to 1,850,000 m). Thus, we must choose the apoapsis distance:

\( r_{a} = 2,050,000 \;{\rm m}\)

which is an altitude of 310,000 m above the surface of the Moon.
Part C: Before separating the Lunar Landing Module (LLM) and beginning the descent to the Moon's surface, the coupled Command Service Module and LLM made one more course correction to circularize the orbit. In order to adjust the orbit into a circle with a radius of 1,850,000 m by making a velocity adjustment at the periapsis of the elliptical orbit of Part B, how much would the Apollo 11 spacecraft have had to slow down? [show]
System: [show system]
The spacecraft will be approximated as a point particle.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The gravitational interaction between the spacecraft and the Moon.

Models: [show Models]
Uniform Circular Motion Model.

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

Find the Speed of the Circular Orbit [show]
The centripetal acceleration required to keep the spacecraft in a circular orbit is provided by the gravitational force acting on it. Thus, we know:

\(ma = m\frac{v^2}{r} = \frac{GMm}{r^{2}}\)

which can be solved for the velocity:

\( v = \sqrt{\frac{GM}{r}} = \sqrt{\frac{(6.67\times 10^{-11}\;\frac{{\rm Nm}^{2}}{{\rm kg}^{2}})\times (7.36\times 10^{22}\;{\rm kg})}{1,850,000\;{\rm m}}} = 1630\;{\rm m/s}\)
Find the Speed Change [show]
The speed of the elliptical orbit of Part B at periapsis was 1670 m/s. The speed needed for a circular orbit at 1,850,000 m is 1630 m/s. Thus, the Apollo 11 spacecraft had to reduce its speed by 40 m/s in order to adjust its orbit.
Understand the Answer [show]
Looking at the data given in Part A and Part B, we find that an engine burn of 360 s resulted in a speed reduction from 2500 m/s to 1670 m/s. If we assume that the mass of the propellant used in such a burn was negligible compared to the mass of the spacecraft, this implies a reduction of (2500 m/s - 1670 m/s)/360 s = 2.3 m/s per second of burn. Thus, to reduce the speed of the craft by an additional 40 m/s would have required a burn of about (40 m/s) / (2.3 m/s/s) = 17 s. According to NASA, the actual burn lasted 16.88 s.
