Illustrative Example: The Galactic Center

This animation was created by Prof. Andrea Ghez and her research team at UCLA and are from data sets obtained with the W.M. Keck Telescopes.

The animation above was constructed by the UCLA Galactic Center Group. It shows the positions of stars near the center of our own Milky Way Galaxy. Several of the stars are clearly following orbital paths with periods on the order of the total observation time (15 years). The star shape at the center of the animation is the best fit to the position of the very massive object whose gravitational attraction is causing the orbital motion, assuming Keplerian orbits. Data for several of these orbits was presented in a 2005 paper by the group and is shown below. In the words of the group:

"These orbits, and a simple application of Kepler's Laws, provide the best evidence yet for a supermassive black hole, which has a mass of 4 million times the mass of the Sun."

Challenge: Using the data in the table above confirm that the mass of the central object has a mass approximately 4 million times that of our Sun. To do this, you will need to know that 1 AU is the mean Earth-Sun separation (1.50×1011 m) and that the mass of the sun is 2.0×1030 kg. [show]
System: [show system]
Each of the stars will be treated as a point particle. The presumed black hole will be treated as an infinitely massive object.

Interactions: [show interactions]
We will assume that the gravity of the black hole is the only relevant interaction experienced by any of the stars.

Model: [show Model]
Keplerian Orbit

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

We simply evaluate Newton's predictive form of Kepler's Third Law for each of the stars in the table solving for M, the mass of the presumed black hole.

Step 1: Convert Units [show]
The hardest part of this problem is keeping the unit conversions straight. Below we summarize the SI unit values for each of the orbital parameters. For this exercise we will not attempt to propagate errors, so they have been dropped from the table.
Star Period (s) Semi-Major Axis (m)
S0-2 4.59×108 1.37×1014
S0-16 1.14×109 2.51×1014
S0-19 1.18×109 2.57×1014
S0-20 1.36×109 2.84×1014
S0-1 6.00×109 7.63×1014
Step 2: Solve the Equation [show]
We can now rearrange the Third Law to solve for the mass M of the black hole:

\( M = \frac{4\pi^{2}a^{3}}{T^{2}G}\)

Step 3: Substitute Numbers [show]
For S0-2, the calculation of M proceeds as:

\( M = \frac{4\times(3.1416)^{2}\times(1.37\times 10^{14}\;{\rm m})^{3}}{(4.59\times 10^{8}\;{\rm s})^{2}\times (6.67\times10^{-11} \frac{{\rm Nm}^{2}}{{\rm kg}^{2}})}=7.3\times 10^{36}\;kg\)

This answer can be converted to solar masses, giving:

\(7.3\times 10^{36}\;kg \times \frac{1 M_{\odot}}{2.0\times 10^{30}\;kg} \sim 4\times 10^{6}\;M_{\odot}\)

Repeating this process for all the stars confirms the estimate of S0-2.
