Illustrative Example: Halley's Comet

Photo by W. Liller courtesy of NASA's National Space Science Data Center Photo Gallery

Halley's Comet is so named because its relatively short orbital period of 75-76 years allowed British astronomer Edmond Halley in 1705 to predict the comet's 1758 reappearance. Its last solar periapsis occurred in 1986, and the next will occur in 2061.

Part A: At periapsis (generally called perihelion for bodies orbiting the sun) Halley's comet is only 0.59 AU from the sun and moves at a speed of 55 km/s. What is the distance from the sun at apoapsis (known as aphelion)? [show]
System: [show system]
The comet will be approximated as a point particle and the Sun as an infinitely massive object.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The gravitational interaction between the comet and Sun.

Models: [show Models]
Keplerian Orbit Model.

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

Understand the Givens [show]
We have the periapsis distance and velocity, but we know neither the apoapsis distance nor the apoapsis speed. Thus, we do not have enough information to apply conservation of angular momentum to this problem. We do, however, know that the comet is famous for its short period of 76 years. Since the periapsis distance and apoapsis distance are related to the semi-major axis of the orbit, we can use Newton's form of Kepler's Third Law to solve for the semi-major axis and then derive the apoapsis distance.
Find the Semi-Major Axis [show]
Solving Kepler's Third Law for the semi-major axis gives:

\( a = \left( \frac{GMT^{2}}{4\pi^{2}}\right)^{1/3} \)

where M will be the mass of the sun (2.0×1030 kg). We can then substitute numbers, remembering to convert units:

\( a=\Bigg( \frac{6.67\times 10^{-11} \frac{{\rm Nm}^{2}}{{\rm kg}^2} }{4\times 3.1416^{2}} \times(2.0\times 10^{30}\;{\rm kg}) \times(2.4\times 10^{9}\;{\rm s})^{2} \Bigg)^{1/3} = 2.7\times 10^{12}\;{\rm m} = 18 AU\)
Determine Apoapsis [show]
Remembering the relationship that the periapsis distance plus the apoapsis distance must equal the full major axis distance, we find the apoapsis distance to be:

\( r_{a} = 2a - r_{p} = 2\times (18\;{\rm AU}) - 0.59\;{\rm AU} = 35\;{\rm AU}\)
Understand the Answer [show]
The orbit of Pluto has a periapsis distance of about 30 AU and an apoapsis distance of about 49 AU from the Sun. Thus the apoapsis location of Halley's Comet is comparable to the location of Pluto.
Part B: What is the speed of Halley's Comet at apoapsis? [show]
System: [show system]
The comet will be approximated as a point particle and the Sun as an infinitely massive object. Angular quantities will be calculated about an axis of rotation passing through the Sun perpendicular to the plane of the comet's motion.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The gravitational interaction between the comet and Sun produces no torque about the chosen axis. Thus, there are no relevant interactions.

Models: [show Models]
Constant Angular Momentum Model.

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

Solve for Apoapsis Speed [show]
The relationship implied by angular momentum conservation is:

\(v_{a} = v_{p}\frac{r_{p}}{r_{a}}\)
No Need to Convert [show]
Since the periapsis and apoapsis distances appear in a ratio, the units will cancel. Thus, we can use any convenient set of units:

\( v_{a} = (55\;{\rm km/s})\times \left(\frac{0.59\;{\rm AU}}{35\;{\rm AU}}\right) = 0.93\;{\rm km/s} \)
Understand the Answer [show]
The speed of 930 m/s is comparable to that of a supersonic jet. The space shuttle in orbit travels approximately 7.8 km/s, and the earth travels about 30 km/s in its orbit around the sun.
