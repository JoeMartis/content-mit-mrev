Uniform Gravity near the Earth

For objects near the surface of the Earth (this includes any object a non-astronaut will ever encounter) the gravitational interaction between the Earth and the object results in a force that is called the weight of the object. In general, objects are subject to a gravitational force that decreases with their separation from the Earth as discussed in the next section, but weight near the surface can be accurately modeled as follows:

The weight of an object always points straight down (where "down" means toward the center of the earth) and has a magnitude proportional to the object's mass (m). In the International System of Units, mass is measured in kilograms (kg) and the constant of proportionality is g = 9.8 m/s2.

 The mathematical representation (or the Force Law) of the magnitude of this force is given by:


\( \mathcal{} W=mg\)

where

\(\mathcal{}g = 9.8\mbox{ m/s}^{2}\).

 

The free body diagram for any object of mass m near the Earth's surface will always contain this downward-pointing force.
