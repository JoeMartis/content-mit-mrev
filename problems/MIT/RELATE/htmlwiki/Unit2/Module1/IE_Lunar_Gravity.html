Illustrative Example: The Acceleration of Gravity Near the Surface of the Moon

Consider an object of mass m at the surface of the Moon.  By analogy with the weight of an object near the Earth's surface, we model the force exerted on the object by the Moon as pointing down towards the Moon's center with a magnitude:

\(\mathcal{}F_{mM} = mg_{M}  \)(eq. 1)

where gM is the acceleration due gravity at the Moon's surface. Our goal is to find gM.

We treat both the object and the Moon as uniform spheres with masses \(m\) and \(M\), respectively, and denote their positions by the centers of each sphere. With this assumption we can use the Law of Universal Gravitation to find the force exerted on the object by the Moon.

\(F_{mM}= G \frac{mM}{r_{mM}^{2}}\) (eq. 2)


where rmM is the distance between the center of the object and the center of the Moon. Since we are interested in the near-Moon-surface force, it is reasonable to approximate this distance by the radius of the Moon, RM (can you see why?) Thus we will set rmM = RM.


Equating (eq. 1 and 2) we have:

\( mg_{M} = G \frac{mM}{R_{M}^{2}}\)


Canceling m we obtain:

\( g_{M} = G \frac{M}{R_{M}^{2}} \) (eq. 3)


Substituting the values of the Moon's mass, M = 7.3477 × 1022 kg, and the value of the Moon's radius, RM = 1,737.10 km, in (eq. 3) we get:


gM = 1.6 m/s2


This value is about 1/6 of the acceleration due to gravity at the Earth's surface (9.8 m/s2)

The expression of the acceleration due to gravity in (eq. 3) is valid at the surface of ANY spherical object with its mass uniformly distributed in volume.
