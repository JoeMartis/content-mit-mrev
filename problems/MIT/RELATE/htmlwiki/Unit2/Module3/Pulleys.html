Idealized Massless Pulleys

We will often incorporate pulleys into problems with ropes or strings. For now, when you see a pulley in a picture you should treat it is as massless. This assumption means it will require no work to rotate or translate (read: move in a straight line) the pulley, the pulley changes only the direction of the rope. Later in the course, when we have learned about rotational motion, we will be able to discuss the effects of massive pulleys. Pulleys are commonly encountered in two qualitatively different types of situation.

Example 1: Pulley Fixed to a Surface [show]
In the figure, box 1 is connected to box 2 by rope R passing over a pulley and is moving along a horizontal and smooth table (t). The idealized massless pulley fixed to a surface acts only to change the direction of the rope and, subsequently, the direction of the tension force applied to the objects at either end of the rope.

Example 2: Pulley Fixed to an Object [show]
An idealized massless pulley fixed to an object whose motion we are analyzing not only changes the direction of the tension in the rope, but also increases the number of effective contact points the object has with the rope . Every point in the rope at which it comes on or off the pulley counts as a separate contact point between the object and the rope in the free body diagram of the object.
