Interactions and Force

Interactions and the forces they produce lie at the heart of Newtonian mechanics because the vector sum of the forces acting on a body accounts for any changes its motion. These relationships may be summarized:

		Interactions --> Forces --> Changes of Motion

Learning Goals

By the end of this unit, you should be able to:

  • Identify the interactions present for an object in a given physical situation,
  • Identify and quantify the resulting forces on the object; draw the corresponding free body diagram, checking that the forces satisfy Newton’s 2nd Law.
  • Know which interactions obey force laws, and know the force laws for gravity, springs, and friction

Physical Interactions and Forces

An interaction involves a particular physical mechanism that occurs between two different objects. All interactions generate a third law pair of forces between the two bodies that interact via the particular physical mechanism. In other words, when two objects interact with each other they will exert a force on each other according to Newton's 3rd law.

Illustrative Examples

A book at rest on a horizontal table [show]
The book in the figure interacts with two objects: the Earth and the table.


As a result of the book – Earth interaction there is a third law pair of forces:


\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{bE} \): the gravitational force exerted on the book by the Earth,

\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{Eb} \): the gravitational force exerted on the Earth by the book.

 

The book - table interaction produces another third law pairr:


\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{bt} \): the normal force exerted on the book by the table,

\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{tb} \): the normal force exerted on the table by the book.


The free body diagram of the book, the right figure, shows the forces applied on the book as a result of its interactions with the Earth and the table. Notice ONLY the forces acting on the book are included in the FBD of the book.
A box sliding down a smooth incline [show]
In this example we consider a box sliding down a smooth incline as shown in the figure. During the motion down, the box interacts with two objects: the Earth and the surface of the incline.


As a result of the box – Earth interaction there is a third law pair of forces:


\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{bE} \mbox{: }\) the force exerted on the box by the Earth,

\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{Eb} \mbox{: }\) the force exerted on the Earth by the box.


The box - surface interaction produces another third law pair:


\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{bS} \mbox{: }\) the normal force exerted on the box by the surface,

\(\mathcal{}\vec{F}_{Sb} \mbox{: }\) the normal force exerted on the surface by the box.


The above two forces are the result of a direct contact between the surfaces of the box and the incline. Because the box moves along a smooth surface we can neglect friction.The two contact forces, FbS and FSb, are perpendicular (also called "normal") to both surfaces.


The free body diagram of the box, the right figure, shows the forces applied on the box as a result of its interactions with the Earth and the incline surface, when friction is negligible ("smooth" surfaces usually modeled using this approximation).

Interactions studied in this class

In this unit, we introduce the four classes of interactions which are frequently encountered in introductory mechanics:

  • uniform gravitational force at the surface of the earth (a special case of Newton’s universal gravitation); commonly called "weight"
  • spring force due to elastic compression or extension of an object (e.g. spring) which attempts to restore its original configuration.
  • tension force by a solid object like a rod or string when stretched along the linear direction of the rod or string
  • contact forces between solid objects; static and kinetic friction forces as well as "normal" forces
