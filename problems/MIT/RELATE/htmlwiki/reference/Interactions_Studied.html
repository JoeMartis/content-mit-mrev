In mechanics, an interaction is said to be present whenever one object has the capacity to influence the motion of another object. Note that this definition requires the presence of two objects in order for an interaction to be present. In physics, all interactions are mutual in the sense that if one object has the capacity to influence the motion of a second object, the second also has that same capacity to influence the motion of the first. This mutuality is quantitatively codified in Newton's Third Law of Motion.

In this course we will study four common types of interaction.

  • Contact (two objects are in direct contact at their surface).
  • Gravity (action-at-a-distance between any two massive objects).
  • Tension (two objects are connected by a rope or string or cable).
  • Elastic Restoring (two objects are connected by a spring).

Description of Interactions

Interactions may be described differently depending on the model chosen to describe the changes in a system manifested or revealed by the interactions.

  • In physical models that describe translational motion, the fundamental physical aspect of the interaction is best described by force, which can be used to define alternate manifestations of the interaction as acceleration or impulse.
  • In physical models that describe rotational motion, the fundamental physical aspect of interactions will be revealed by describing torque, or alternately angular acceleration and angular impulse.
  • Where both translational and rotational motion are involved, the more complex mechanical energy model describes interactions in terms of system energy states and energy changes. In this model both force and torque can play a role in describing how the energy state is changed through work. Further, interactions with certain mathematical properties are often treated by defining the potential energy state of the system instead of the change in state of the system.
