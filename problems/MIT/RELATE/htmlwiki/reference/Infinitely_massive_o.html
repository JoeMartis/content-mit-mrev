Assuming that an object has infinite mass implies that the other objects in the system are incapable of producing a measurable motion of this object. The approximation will generally be applied to the earth itself or to fixtures (like walls, ceilings, or ramps) that are anchored to the earth.

Momentum or Angular Momentum and Infinitely Massive Objects

Infinitely massive objects should be excluded from the system if momentum or angular momentum will be used to describe the motion. The reason is that infinitely massive objects will destroy momentum conservation if they participate in a collision. Any interactions with the infinitely massive object should be treated as external forces or external torques.

Mechanical Energy and Infinitely Massive Objects

In contrast to their disruptive role in momentum conservation, infinitely massive objects do not create problems for energy conservation. Thus, they can be included in the system if mechanical energy is used to describe the motion. This is particularly important when the earth's gravity is relevant. Only internal interactions can be represented by a potential energy. Thus, in order to use potential energy to describe the effects of earth's gravity, the earth must technically be included in the system.
