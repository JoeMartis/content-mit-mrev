Newton's cradle balls Newton's cradle, an ingenious device named after Sir Isaac Newton, consists of five identical metal balls suspended in a frame so that they are just touching each other at rest. Each ball is attached to the frame by two wires of equal length angled away from each other. This restricts the pendulums' movements to the same plane. As shown in the animation, when one ball (ball 1) is pulled away and is let to fall, it strikes the first ball in the series and comes to nearly a dead stop. The ball on the opposite side (ball 5) almost instantly swings out. The device is also known as the Executive Stess Reliever.
This is a file from the Wikimedia Commons

Note that the intermediate balls appear stationary. In fact, the cradle continues to work even if the intermediate balls are physically clamped still. This is also counter intuitive - transmitting motion without moving. What actually happens is that the first impact produces a shock wave that propagates through the intermediate balls. Any efficiently elastic material such as steel will do this as long as the kinetic energy is temporarily stored as potential energy in the compression of the material rather than being lost as heat.

Let's assume that the balls have mass m and are made of a perfectly elastic material. Is it ever possible possible that when ball 1 is released, it stops after the collision and balls 4 and 5 swing out on the opposite side with half of the speed of ball 1?

Answer [show]
NO
Explanation [show]
Let's consider the time interval that starts at the instant ti, right before ball 1 hits the second ball with a horizontal velocity of magnitude v, and ends at the instant tf, right when balls 4 and 5 swing out with a horizontal velocity of magnitude v/2.


System: [SHOW System]
The 5 balls treatead as point particles.

Interactions: [SHOW Interactions]

External:

  • Balls ↔ Earth.
  • Balls ↔ strings.

Internal: The interaction between two consecutive balls. Because we assume that the balls are made of a perfect elastic material these interactions are conservative and are represented by a potential energy.

Because the motion of the balls during the time interval under consideration is horizontal and the external forces of tension and gravity are perpendicular to the balls' displacement, therefore they do not do work. As a result, the mechanical energy of the system is constant.

In addition, during the time interval under consideration, there is no external force along the horizontal direction, therefore the linear momentum of the system is constant.


Model(s): [SHOW Model]
Constant mechanical energy and constant linear momentum applied to the 5 balls system.

Approach:[SHOW]

In order to answer the question we need to check if the linear momentum along the horizontal direction and the mechanical energy of the system are constant during the time interval ti < t < tf. Let's consider a coordinate system with the x-axis horizontal and positive to the right.

Initial State:

  • Ball 1 moving horizontally at speed v.
  • Balls 2 to 5 are at rest.
  • None of the balls are compressing each other (zero elastic potential energy).

The initial momentum of the system along the x-direction:

\(p(t_i)=\;mv\)       (eq. 1)

The initial mechanical energy is only the kinetic of ball 1:

\(E(t_i) = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\)   (eq. 2)

Final State:

  • Balls 4 & 5 moving together horizontally at speed v/2
  • Balls 1 to 3 are at rest.
  • None of the balls are compressing each other (no elastic potential energy).

The final momentum of the system along the x-direction:

\(p(t_f)=m\frac{v}{2}+m\frac{v}{2} = mv\)        (eq. 3)

The final mechanical energy is the kinetic of balls 4 & 5:

\(E(t_f) = \frac{1}{2}m(v/2)^2 + \frac{1}{2}m(v/2)^2 = m\frac{v^2}{4}\)    (eq. 4)

By comparing (eq. 1) and (eq. 3) we find that the linear momentum is constant during the collisions, but by comparing (eq. 2) and (eq. 4) we found that the mechanical energy is not constant. Then the event of balls 4 & 5 moving at speed v/2 after ball 1 hits ball 2 with speed v and stops is not possible.
