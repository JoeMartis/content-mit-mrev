Illustrative Example: A collision of two moving objects
Consider two objects of mass m1 and m2 moving on a horizontal surface with velocities \(\vec{v}_1\) and \(\vec{v}_2\), (\(|\vec{v}_1|>|\vec{v}_2|\)) . What are the velocities of the the objects \(\vec{v}_1^'\) and \(\vec{v}_2^'\) right after the collision? Assume the collision is perfectly ellastc.

Note: It can be shown that in a prefect elastic collision, the relative velocity of object 2 with respect to object 1 before the collision is equal to minus the the relative velocity of object 2 with respect to object 1 after the collision: \(\vec{v}_2-\vec{v_1}=-(\vec{v}_2^'-\vec{v}_1')\)

System: [SHOW System]
The two objets

Interactions: [SHOW Interactions]

External:

  • Object 1 & 2 ↔ Earth.
  • Object 1 & 2 ↔ surface.

The resulting external forces in the vertical direction are gravity and normal. These forces cancel each other. In addition, these forces do not do work on the system because they are perpendicular to the objects' displacement.

Short time collision: We will assume that the collision lasts a short time, therefore if there is any friction along the surface we will neglect its impulse. Under the same assumption, if the time interval of the collision is small, there is no time for the objects to move while they are in contact (no displacement), then the external friction will not do work.

We can then assume that:

  • the net external impulse is zero, then the linear momentum of the system is constant, and
  • the external forces do not do work.

Internal:

Object 1 ↔ Object 2. The resulting collision forces are assumed to be conservative.

  • Internal forces are conservative and the external forces do not do work, then the mechanical energy of the system is constant.

Models: [SHOW Models]
Contstant Linear Momentum and Constant Mechanical Energy.

Approach:[SHOW]

We will consider the time interval ti < t < tf. The initial and final state of the system are represented diagrammatically in the figures below, where the primes denote the velocity of the objects at time tf.

Initial Momentum Final Momentum
\(p(t_i)=m_1\;v_1+m_2\;v_2\) \(p(t_f)=m_1\;v_1^' + m_2\;v_2^'\)
Initial Kinetic Energy Final Kinetic Energy
\(K(t_i)=\frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2+\frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^2\) \(K(t_f)=\frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^{'2}+\frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^{'2}\)

Note that in the momentum equation we are not using the arrow notation. The motion is in 1 dimension, therefore the direction of the velocities is going to be indicated by the algebraic sign in the equations.

Question 1: After the collision object 2 is:
  • moving faster
  • moving slower
  • at rest
  • there is not enough information to decide about its motion
[show]
Answer: Object 2 is moving faster

During the collision the force exerted on object 2 by object 1, F21, is pointing to the right, therefore its associated impulse is to the right. As a result, the final momentum of object 2 is to the right and larger than the initial one as shown in the momentum diagram below.

Question 2: After the collision object 1 is:
  • moving to the left
  • moving to the right
  • at rest
  • not enough information to decide about its motion.
[show]
Answer: There is not enough information to decide

During the collision the force exerted on object 1 by object 2, F12, is pointing to the left, therefore its associated impulse is also pointing to the left. The final momentum of object 1 is obtained as:

\(\vec{p}_1(t_f)=\vec{p}_1(t_i) + \int_{t_i}^{t_f}\vec{F}_{12} dt\)

Considering that the +x axis is pointing to the right and that object 1 is initially moving to the right then the above equation becomes:

\(p_1(t_f)=p_1(t_i) - \int_{t_i}^{t_f}F_{12} dt\)

We note that the right hand side can be positive, zero or negative depending on the magnitudes of the initial momentum and the external impulse on object 1. The possible situations are sketched in the momentum diagram below.

Apply the models:

Using that the momentum and energy are constant during the collision:

\(m_1\;v_1+m_2\;v_2 = m_1\;v_1^'+m_2\;v_2^'\)                (eq. 1)

\(\frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2+\frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^{'2}+\frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^{'2}\)    (eq. 2)

Equations (1) and (2) are the two equations needed to solve for the two unknowns \(v_1^'\) and \(v_2^'\).

The algebra to solve this 2x2 system is shown below. Because the energy equation is quadratic the solution requires a math trick that reduces the quadratic equation to a linear one.

Show algebra: [show]
The trick consists on grouping on the same side of the equations the velocities corresponding to the same object. For example, let's group on the left hand side the velocities corresponding to object 1 and on the right hand side the velocities corresponding to object 2:

\(m_1\; (v_1-v_1^') = m_2 (\;v_2^' - v_2)\)           (eq. 3)

\(\frac{1}{2}m_1(v_1^2-v_1^{'2}) = \frac{1}{2}m_2(v_2^{'2}-v_2^2)\)    (eq. 4)

The trick continues by realizing that there is a difference of two squares on each side of (eq. 4), therefore we can rewrite (eq. 4) as:

\(m_1(v_1-v_1^')(v_1+v_1^') = m_2(v_2^'- v_2)(v_2' + v_2)\)   (eq. 5)

where we have canceled the 1/2. Using (eq. 3) on the left hand side of (eq. 5) we obtain:

\(m_2(v_2^' - v_2)(v_1+v_1^') = m_2(v_2^'-v_2)(v_2' + v_2)\)   (eq. 6)


After canceling \(m_2(v_2^'- v_2)\) from both sides, (eq. 6) becomes a linear equation:

\(v_1+v_1^' = v_2' + v_2 \)     (eq. 7)

Now we have to solve a system of two linear equations, the transformed momentum equation, (eq. 3), and the transformed energy equation given by (eq. 7):

\(m_1(v_1-v_1^') = m_2(v_2^'- v_2)\)           (eq. 3)

\(v_1+v_1^' = v_2' + v_2 \)                           (eq. 7)


Solve (eq. 3) and (eq. 7):


For final velocity of object 1:

Multiply (eq. 7) by m2 and subtract it to (eq. 3):

\(m_2\times\mbox{(eq. 7)}-\mbox{(eq. 3)}\)

After rearranging we obtain: \((m_2-m_1)v_1+(m_2+m_1)v_1^'= 2m_2v_2\)

and,

\(v_1^' =\frac{m_1-m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{2m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)


For the final velocity of object 2:

Multiply (eq. 7) by m1 and add it to (eq. 3):

\(m_1\times\mbox{(eq. 7)}+\mbox{(eq. 3)}\)

After rearranging we obtain:

\(2m_1v_1=(m_1+m_2)v_2^'+(m_1-m_2)v_2\)

and,

\(v_2^'=\frac{2m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{m_2-m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)

Relative velocities before and after the collision:

Moving around the terms of (eq. 7) we obtain:

\(v_2 - v_1 = -( v_2' - v_1^') \)

The relative velocity of 2 with respect to 1 before the collision is minus the relative velocity of 2 with respect to 1 after the collision.

After solving the equations (open the window bove) we obtain:

Object 1 \(v_{1}^'=\frac{m_1-m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{2m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)
Object 2 \(v_{2}^' =\frac{2m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{m_2-m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)
Note 1: To apply the above equations remember to use the proper sign for the initial velocities of the objects to indicate the direction of motion.
Relative velocities \(v_2 - v_1 = -( v_2' - v_1^') \)
Note 2: The relative velocity of 2 with respect to 1 before the collision is minus the relative velocity of 2 with respect to 1 after the collision.
