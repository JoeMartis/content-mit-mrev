A ball hits a rod
A ball of mass m and speed vi is moving on a horizontal and frictionless surface along the +x-axis. The ball hits the end of a rod that is at rest on the surface as shown. The figure is a top view of the situation described (gravity is into the screen). The rod is uniform, has a mass m and a length d. After the collision, the ball continues moving along the +x axis with 80% of its initial speed.

Is there any energy loss during the collision? If so, calculate the loss.

Solution: [show]
To answer the question we need to compare the energy available before the collision (kinetic energy of the ball) with the energy after the collision (kinetic energy of the ball plus the kinetic energy -translational + rotational - of the rod).


System: [SHOW System]

The ball treated as a point particle and the rod treated as a rigid object.


Interactions: [SHOW Interactions]
  • Internal:

ball ↔ rod. The resulting forces are the unknown collision forces. These forces will not change the linear and the angular momentum of the system.

The internal collision forces might not be conservative. In this case, they will do non-conservative work changing the mechanical energy of the system.

  • External:

ball & rod ↔ Earth. The resulting force is the weight on ball & rod.

ball & rod ↔ Surface. Because there is no friction, the resulting contact force is the normal force exerted on the ball & rod by the surface.

1. The external forces are perpendicular to the displacement of the objects so they do not do work.

2. The net external force on the ball-rod system is zero, then the linear momentum of the system is constant.

3. In addition , the net external torque on the system is constant with respect to any point in space, therefore the angular momentum of the system is constant with respect to any point. We will consider the point to be the rod's center of mass, point Q in the figure.


Note: Do not confuse the center of mass of the rod with the center of mass of the system

In order to avoid confusion between the center of mass of the ball-rod system and the center of mass of the rod, we use the point Q when we refer to the center of mass of the rod and vrod when we refer to the speed of the rod's center of mass.


Models: [SHOW Models]
  • Constant Linear Momentum
  • Constant Angular Momentum about the center of mass of the rod.
  • Mechanical Energy, External Work, and Internal-Non Conservative work.

The three models are applied to the ball-rod system.


Approach: [SHOW]

We will consider the collision to last a time interval defined as: ti < t < tf.

The initial and final states are summarized in the figures below.

ti: Instant right before the ball hits the rod. tf: Instant right after the ball looses contact with the rod.
The ball is moving to right with speed vi The ball is moving to the right with speed vf = 0.8vi
The rod is at rest. The rod is translating with speed vrod and rotating clockwise with angular speed ω

We are describing the motion of the objects with respect to a coordinate system fixed at the surface. Its +x-axis is parallel to the ball's direction of motion, and its y axis is along the axis of the rod. When the rod is at rest, its center of mass coincides with origin of the coordinate system.

Apply the models:

Constant Linear Momentum:
The linear momentum of the ball-rod system is constant:


\(mv_i\hat{i}=m\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{i}+m\vec{v}_{rod}\)    (eq. 1)

Show derivation of (eq. 1): [show]
The initial momentum:

Only the ball is moving, then the initial linear momentum of the system is the one of the ball:

\(\vec{P}^{sys}(t_i)=mv_i\hat{i}\)


The final momentum: Right after the collision, the ball and the rod are moving. The momentum of the system is the sum of momentum of the ball and the rod:

\(\vec{P}^{sis}(t_f)=mv_f\hat{i}+\vec{v}_{rod}= m\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{i}+\vec{v}_{rod}\)


The linear momentum is constant, implies:

\(mv_i\hat{i}=m\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{i}+m\vec{v}_{rod}\)

Constant Angular Momentum about point Q:
The angular momentum of the system about point Q is constant implies:


\(-m\frac{d}{2}v_i\;\hat{k}=-m\frac{d}{2}\mbox{0.8}v_i\;\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\;\hat{k}\)    (eq. 2)

where \(I_Q\;\) is the moment of inertia of the rod about its center of mass.

Show derivation of (eq. 2): [show]
The initial angular momentum about point Q: Only the ball is moving, then the initial angular momentum is that of one point particle moving along a straight line:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{sys}(t_i)=-m\frac{d}{2}v_i\hat{k}\)

where \(+\hat{k}\) is pointing out of the screen.


The final angular momentum about point Q: Right after the collision, the angular momentum of the system is the sum of the angular momentum of the ball (point particle moving along a straight line) and the angular momentum of a rigid object rotating about an axis passing through its center of mass:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{sys}(t_f)=-m\frac{d}{2}v_f\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\hat{k}\)

where IQ, is the moment of inertia of a uniform rod about its center of mass.

Using that the speed after the collision is 80% of the speed before the collision, the final angular momentum becomes:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{sys}(t_f)=-m\frac{d}{2}\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\hat{k}\)

Note: because we have chosen to calculate the angular momentum with respect to point Q, the rod's center of mass, then the rod's angular momentum only has the spin component.


The angular momentum of the system about point Q is constant implies:


\(-m\frac{d}{2}v_i\;\hat{k}=-m\frac{d}{2}\mbox{0.8}v_i\;\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\;\hat{k}\)

From (eq. 1) we can solve for the velocity of the rod's center of mass:


\(\vec{v}_{rod}=\mbox{0.2}v_i\;\hat{i}\)      (eq. 3)


Using (eq. 2) we obtain the angular speed of the rod:


\(\omega=\frac{md\;\mbox{0.2}\;v_i}{2I_Q}\)

Inserting the value of the moment of inertia of a rod about its center of mass, \(I_Q=\;md^2/12\), in the above equation we obtain:


\(\omega=\mbox{1.2}\frac{v_i}{d}\)    (eq. 4)


Mechanical Energy:

The mechanical energy of the system is only kinetic.


Initial Mechanical energy:

Only the ball is moving:

\(E(t_i)=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2\)   (eq. 5)


Final Mechanical energy:

The ball and the rod are moving:

\(E(t_f)\;=\;K^{ball}(t_f)+K^{rod}(t_F)\)


The rod is a rigid object rotating and translating then its kinetic energy has a translational and rotational component:


\(E(t_f)\;=\Big(\frac{1}{2}m(0.8v_i)^2\Big)+\Big(\frac{1}{2}mv_{rod}^2+\frac{1}{2}I_Q\;\omega^2\Big)\)


Using (eq. 3) and (eq. 4) and the value of the moment of inertia of a rod about its center of mass, the final mechanical energy is given by:

\(E(t_f)\;=\frac{1}{2}m(0.8v_i)^2+\frac{1}{2}m(0.2v_i)^2+\frac{1}{2}(\frac{md^2}{12})(\frac{1.2v_i}{d})^2\)


rearranging terms,


\(E(t_f)\;=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2(0.8^2+0.2^2+\frac{1.2^2}{12})\)

\(E(t_f)\;=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2(0.64+0.04+0.12)=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2\;0.8\)

The energy decreased by 20%:

\((E(t_f)-E(t_i))/E(t_i)\;=\;(0.8-1)=-0.2\)
