Illustrative Example

A ball of mass m attached to a string of length d is set to rotate in a vertical circle. What is the minimum speed that the ball must have at the bottom of the circle so that it can complete one turn.
tf: Instant right when the ball is at the top of the circle moving with speed vf.





ti: Instant right when the ball is at the bottom of the circle moving with speed vi.

This problem is an example of a one step problem solved by applying 3 models:

  • constant mechanical energy (relates the initial and the final state of the problem)
  • dynamics and net force applied at the final state of the problem.
  • circular motion applied at the final state of the problem.
We will consider the time interval ti < t < tf that it takes the ball to move up along the circle. Looking at the system and interactions (SHOW)
System and Interactions:

System: ball treated as a point particle and the Earth treated as a point particle of infinite mass. (By treating the Earth as an object of infinite mass its motion can be neglected when the ball is moving.)

Interactions:

  • Internal: ball interacts with the Earth. This internal interaction is conservative and can be expressed as a potential energy.
  • External: The ball and the string. The resulting force is the force of tension that is perpendicular to the ball's displacement, therefore it does not do work.

Then, the mechanical energy of the ball+Earth system is constant: \(E(t_f) = \; E(t_i)\)

Considering the zero for potential energy at the bottom:

Initial mechanical energy:

\(E(t_i) = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2\)

Final Mechanical Energy:

\(E(t_f) = \frac{1}{2}v_f^2 + mg2d \)

we conclude that during this time interval the mechanical energy of the ball-Earth system is constant:


\(\frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mg2d = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 \;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 1)}\)


To find the speed at the bottom, vi , we need to know the speed at the top, vf .

Condition of minimum speed:

To obtain the speed at the bottom we will use the condition about the minimum speed needed at the bottom in order to make a turn.

Let's consider the ball to be the system. Tension and gravity are the only forces exerted on the ball. At the top of the circle, both forces are pointing towards the center  (SHOW FORCES)
.

Applying the dynamics and net force model and using the coordinate system shown above we have that at the top, \(\sum\vec{F}=m\vec{a}\) becomes:


\(- T - mg = - m\;a_r\)


where ar is the magnitude of the ball's acceleration vector, that at the top, it has only one component that is pointing downwards to the center and is given by:

\(a_r = \frac{v_f^2}{d}\)

Combining both equations we obtain:

\(T + mg = m\frac{v_f^2}{d}\)

Applying the dynamics and net force and circular motion models, we obtain a relationship between the magnitude of the tension on the string and the ball's speed at the top of the circle:


\(T + mg = m\frac{v_f^2}{d}\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 2)}\)


The ball will be able to make one turn if T = 0 at the top. (SHOW EXPLANATION)
Understanding the minimum speed condition needed to make a turn:

From the energy equation, (eq. 1), the slower the ball is moving at the bottom, the slower moves at the top, and, from (eq. 2) the smaller vf, smaller is the tension in the string, T. The minimum value that the tension can have at the top is zero. When T = 0 at the top, the ball will just have enough speed to complete a turn. If the tension achieves a value of zero before reaching the top then the ball will not make a complete turn.

Using the condition T = 0 in (eq. 2) we obtain that in order to make a complete turn, the ball must have at the top a speed given by:

\(v_f=\sqrt{gd} \)

Replacing this expression in (eq. 1) we obtain that the minimum speed the ball needs to have at the bottom in order to do a complete turn is:

\(v_i=\sqrt{5gd}\)
