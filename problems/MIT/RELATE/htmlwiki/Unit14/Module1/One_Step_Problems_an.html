In a one step problem the interactions that produce change in the system are the same during the time interval it takes the system to go from its initial to its final state.

The simplest of these type of problems are those that are solved by applying one single model. For example, calculate the speed of a given object at the bottom of a smooth incline after the object has been released from rest from the top of the incline. Using constant mechanical energy model we can obtain the answer.

In this module we will illustrate one step problems that are solved by applying, during the time interval under consideration, more than one model simultaneously. For example, in the problem below, the state of the system formed by the two boxes and the spring changes under the action of internal elastic forces. As a result, the constant momentum and the constant energy models are used simultaneously. Another example that you have already seen several times is the one of projectile motion. While the object is in air, it interacts with Earth only. As a result, the problems are solved by using the constant acceleration and the constant velocity models simultaneously.

Illustrative Example 1

Two identical boxes of mass m are placed in such a way to compress a spring a distance d from its equilibrium position. The spring is massless, has a spring constant k, and it is not attached to block 1. A rope is used to keep the blocks together. Both blocks are initially at rest. The rope is cut. Find the speed of box 1, v1, right at the instant when it loses contact with the spring.
ti : instant right after cutting the rope tf : instant right after 1 looses contact with the spring

We will consider the time interval ti < t < tf which corresponds to the time it takes the spring to expand from its initial compression of d until it pushes both boxes away loosing contact with box 1.

Looking at the system and interactions (SHOW)
System and Interactions:

System: The two boxes, treated as point particles, and the massless spring.

Interactions:

External:

  • The boxes and the Earth (the spring is massless so it does not interact with the Earth): gravitational force on both boxes.
  • The boxes and the frictionless surface: normal force on both boxes.

These external forces cancel each other:

  • \(\sum_{ext}\vec{F} = 0\)

and, the work done by the external forces is zero

  • \(W_{i,f}^{ext} = 0\)

Internal:

  • The spring interacting with Box1 and Box 2. The results of these interactions is a conservative force that can be represented as elastic potential energy.
we conclude that during this time interval the external and internal forces of the 2 boxes + spring system are such that:
1. Net External force 2. External work 3. Internal forces:
\(\sum_{ext}\vec{F} = 0\) \(W_{i,f}^{ext} = 0\) CONSERVATIVE

Then for ti < t < tf both the linear momentum (1) and the mechanical energy (2 and 3) of the 2 boxes + spring system are constant.

This problem is an example of a one step problem that is solved by applying two models simultaneously: constant momentum and constant mechanical energy.


Solution [show]
We will consider a coordinate system with the x-axis parallel to the surface and positive to the right:

1. Constant Linear momentum apply to the 2 boxes + spring system:

Initial momentum:

\(\vec{p}(t_i)= 0\)

Final momentum:

\(\vec{p}(t_f)= -mv_1\hat{i} + mv_2\hat{i}\)

Because it is constant:

\(\;\;\; \vec{p}(t_f)= \vec{p}(t_i)\)

\(-m\; v_1 + m\;v_2 = 0\;\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 1)}\)

2. Constant Mechanical Energy apply to the 2 boxes + spring system.

Initial mechanical energy:

\(E(t_i) = \frac{1}{2}kd^2\)

Final mechanical energy:

\(E(t_f) = \frac{1}{2}mv_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2\)

Because it is constant:

\( E(t_f) = \;E(t_i) \)

\(\frac{1}{2}mv_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2 = \frac{1}{2}kd^2 \;\;\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 2)}\)

From (eq. 1) we obtain that the speed of both boxes are the same. Replacing this result in (eq. 2), then:

\(\frac{1}{2}mv_1^2 + \frac{1}{2}mv_2^2 =\frac{1}{2}2mv_1^2 = mv_1^2= \frac{1}{2}kd^2\)

\(v_1 =\sqrt{\frac{k}{2m}}d\)

Note: In this example the mechanical energy is constant but the kinetic energy of the system increases, initially was zero and finally it is not. The source of the kinetic energy is the potential energy stored initially in the compressed spring.

Illustrative Example 2

A ball of mass m attached to a string of length d is set to rotate in a vertical circle. What is the minimum speed that the ball must have at the bottom of the circle so that it can complete one turn.
tf: Instant right when the ball is at the top of the circle moving with speed vf.





ti: Instant right when the ball is at the bottom of the circle moving with speed vi.

This problem is an example of a one step problem solved by applying 3 models:

  • constant mechanical energy (relates the initial and the final state of the problem)
  • dynamics and net force applied at the final state of the problem.
  • circular motion applied at the final state of the problem.
We will consider the time interval ti < t < tf that it takes the ball to move up along the circle. Looking at the system and interactions (SHOW)
System and Interactions:

System: ball treated as a point particle and the Earth treated as a point particle of infinite mass. (By treating the Earth as an object of infinite mass its motion can be neglected when the ball is moving.)

Interactions:

  • Internal: ball interacts with the Earth. This internal interaction is conservative and can be expressed as a potential energy.
  • External: The ball and the string. The resulting force is the force of tension that is perpendicular to the ball's displacement, therefore it does not do work.

Then, the mechanical energy of the ball+Earth system is constant: \(E(t_f) = \; E(t_i)\)

Considering the zero for potential energy at the bottom:

Initial mechanical energy:

\(E(t_i) = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2\)

Final Mechanical Energy:

\(E(t_f) = \frac{1}{2}v_f^2 + mg2d \)

we conclude that during this time interval the mechanical energy of the ball-Earth system is constant:


\(\frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 + mg2d = \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2 \;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 1)}\)


To find the speed at the bottom, vi , we need to know the speed at the top, vf .

Condition of minimum speed:

To obtain the speed at the bottom we will use the condition about the minimum speed needed at the bottom in order to make a turn.

Let's consider the ball to be the system. Tension and gravity are the only forces exerted on the ball. At the top of the circle, both forces are pointing towards the center  (SHOW FORCES)
.

Applying the dynamics and net force model and using the coordinate system shown above we have that at the top, \(\sum\vec{F}=m\vec{a}\) becomes:


\(- T - mg = - m\;a_r\)


where ar is the magnitude of the ball's acceleration vector, that at the top, it has only one component that is pointing downwards to the center and is given by:

\(a_r = \frac{v_f^2}{d}\)

Combining both equations we obtain:

\(T + mg = m\frac{v_f^2}{d}\)

Applying the dynamics and net force and circular motion models, we obtain a relationship between the magnitude of the tension on the string and the ball's speed at the top of the circle:


\(T + mg = m\frac{v_f^2}{d}\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 2)}\)


The ball will be able to make one turn if T = 0 at the top. (SHOW EXPLANATION)
Understanding the minimum speed condition needed to make a turn:

From the energy equation, (eq. 1), the slower the ball is moving at the bottom, the slower moves at the top, and, from (eq. 2) the smaller vf, smaller is the tension in the string, T. The minimum value that the tension can have at the top is zero. When T = 0 at the top, the ball will just have enough speed to complete a turn. If the tension achieves a value of zero before reaching the top then the ball will not make a complete turn.

Using the condition T = 0 in (eq. 2) we obtain that in order to make a complete turn, the ball must have at the top a speed given by:

\(v_f=\sqrt{gd} \)

Replacing this expression in (eq. 1) we obtain that the minimum speed the ball needs to have at the bottom in order to do a complete turn is:

\(v_i=\sqrt{5gd}\)

A perfect elastic collision in one dimension [show]
If you know what is a perfect elastic collision you can skip the introduction and solve the problems 1 and 2.
Introduction [show]
In a perfect elastic collision, the forces resulting from the interaction between the two objects are conservative. As a result, the mechanical energy of the system consisting of the two objects is constant during the collision.

Right after a collision of two perfect elastic objects, their shapes start to deform and their kinetic energy before impact is stored into elastic potential energy. When the objects start to recover their original shape, they push away from each other and at the instant right when they loose contact, their original shape is completely restored and all the potential energy is transformed back to kinetic energy. Therefore, the kinetic energy of the system right before the collision is the same as the kinetic energy right after the collision.

Answer: [show]
No. The speed right before the collision is 7 m/s and right after the collision is 6 m/s. The kinetic energy of the tennis ball decreases during the collision. In the picture below, each time the ball bounces, it deforms and loses energy during the collision. As a result it is unable to regain its previous heights.

Solution: [show]
System: [SHOW System]
The two objets

Interactions: [SHOW Interactions]

External:

  • Object 1 & 2 ↔ Earth.
  • Object 1 & 2 ↔ surface.

The resulting external forces in the vertical direction are gravity and normal. These forces cancel each other. In addition, these forces do not do work on the system because they are perpendicular to the objects' displacement.

Short time collision: We will assume that the collision lasts a short time, therefore if there is any friction along the surface we will neglect its impulse. Under the same assumption, if the time interval of the collision is small, there is no time for the objects to move while they are in contact (no displacement), then the external friction will not do work.

We can then assume that:

  • the net external impulse is zero, then the linear momentum of the system is constant, and
  • the external forces do not do work.

Object 1 ↔ Object 2. The resulting collision forces are assumed to be conservative.

Contstant Linear Momentum and Constant Mechanical Energy.

Considering that the +x axis is pointing to the right and that object 1 is initially moving to the right then the above equation becomes:

\(p_1(t_f)=p_1(t_i) - \int_{t_i}^{t_f}F_{12} dt\)

We note that the right hand side can be positive, zero or negative depending on the magnitudes of the initial momentum and the external impulse on object 1. The possible situations are sketched in the momentum diagram below.

Apply the models:

Using that the momentum and energy are constant during the collision:

\(m_1\;v_1+m_2\;v_2 = m_1\;v_1^'+m_2\;v_2^'\)                (eq. 1)

\(\frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^2+\frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^2 = \frac{1}{2}m_1v_1^{'2}+\frac{1}{2}m_2v_2^{'2}\)    (eq. 2)

Equations (1) and (2) are the two equations needed to solve for the two unknowns \(v_1^'\) and \(v_2^'\).

The algebra to solve this 2x2 system is shown below. Because the energy equation is quadratic the solution requires a math trick that reduces the quadratic equation to a linear one.

Show algebra: [show]
The trick consists on grouping on the same side of the equations the velocities corresponding to the same object. For example, let's group on the left hand side the velocities corresponding to object 1 and on the right hand side the velocities corresponding to object 2:

\(m_1\; (v_1-v_1^') = m_2 (\;v_2^' - v_2)\)           (eq. 3)

\(\frac{1}{2}m_1(v_1^2-v_1^{'2}) = \frac{1}{2}m_2(v_2^{'2}-v_2^2)\)    (eq. 4)

The trick continues by realizing that there is a difference of two squares on each side of (eq. 4), therefore we can rewrite (eq. 4) as:

\(m_1(v_1-v_1^')(v_1+v_1^') = m_2(v_2^'- v_2)(v_2' + v_2)\)   (eq. 5)

where we have canceled the 1/2. Using (eq. 3) on the left hand side of (eq. 5) we obtain:

\(m_2(v_2^' - v_2)(v_1+v_1^') = m_2(v_2^'-v_2)(v_2' + v_2)\)   (eq. 6)


After canceling \(m_2(v_2^'- v_2)\) from both sides, (eq. 6) becomes a linear equation:

\(v_1+v_1^' = v_2' + v_2 \)     (eq. 7)

Now we have to solve a system of two linear equations, the transformed momentum equation, (eq. 3), and the transformed energy equation given by (eq. 7):

\(m_1(v_1-v_1^') = m_2(v_2^'- v_2)\)           (eq. 3)

\(v_1+v_1^' = v_2' + v_2 \)                                   (eq. 7)


Solve (eq. 3) and (eq. 7):


For final velocity of object 1:

Multiply (eq. 7) by m2 and subtract it to (eq. 3):

\(m_2\times\mbox{(eq. 7)}-\mbox{(eq. 3)}\)

After rearranging we obtain: \((m_2-m_1)v_1+(m_2+m_1)v_1^'= 2m_2v_2\)

and,

\(v_1^' =\frac{m_1-m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{2m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)


For the final velocity of object 2:

Multiply (eq. 7) by m1 and add it to (eq. 3):

\(m_1\times\mbox{(eq. 7)}+\mbox{(eq. 3)}\)

After rearranging we obtain:

\(2m_1v_1=(m_1+m_2)v_2^'+(m_1-m_2)v_2\)

and,

\(v_2^'=\frac{2m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{m_2-m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)

After solving the equations we obtain:

Object 1 \(v_{1}^'=\frac{m_1-m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{2m_2}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)
Object 2 \(v_{2}^' =\frac{2m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_1+\frac{m_2-m_1}{m_1+m_2}v_2\)
Note: To apply the above equations remember to use the proper sign for the initial velocities of the objects to indicate the direction of motion.

Newton's cradle balls [show]
Newton's cradle, an ingenious device named after Sir Isaac Newton, consists of five identical metal balls suspended in a frame so that they are just touching each other at rest. Each ball is attached to the frame by two wires of equal length angled away from each other. This restricts the pendulums' movements to the same plane. As shown in the animation, when one ball (ball 1) is pulled away and is let to fall, it strikes the first ball in the series and comes to nearly a dead stop. The ball on the opposite side (ball 5) almost instantly swings out. The device is also known as the Executive Stess Reliever.
This is a file from the Wikimedia Commons

Note that the intermediate balls appear stationary. In fact, the cradle continues to work even if the intermediate balls are physically clamped still. This is also counter intuitive - transmitting motion without moving. What actually happens is that the first impact produces a shock wave that propagates through the intermediate balls. Any efficiently elastic material such as steel will do this as long as the kinetic energy is temporarily stored as potential energy in the compression of the material rather than being lost as heat.

Let's assume that the balls have mass m and are made of a perfectly elastic material. Is it ever possible possible that when ball 1 is released, it stops after the collision and balls 4 and 5 swing out on the opposite side with half of the speed of ball 1?

Answer [show]
NO
Explanation [show]
Let's consider the time interval that starts at the instant ti, right before ball 1 hits the second ball with a horizontal velocity of magnitude v, and ends at the instant tf, right when balls 4 and 5 swing out with a horizontal velocity of magnitude v/2.


System: [SHOW System]
The 5 balls treatead as point particles.

Interactions: [SHOW Interactions]

External:

  • Balls ↔ Earth.
  • Balls ↔ strings.

Internal: The interaction between two consecutive balls. Because we assume that the balls are made of a perfect elastic material these interactions are conservative and are represented by a potential energy.

Because the motion of the balls during the time interval under consideration is horizontal and the external forces of tension and gravity are perpendicular to the balls' displacement, therefore they do not do work. As a result, the mechanical energy of the system is constant.

In addition, during the time interval under consideration, there is no external force along the horizontal direction, therefore the linear momentum of the system is constant.


Model(s): [SHOW Model]
Constant mechanical energy and constant linear momentum applied to the 5 balls system.

Approach:[SHOW]

In order to answer the question we need to check if the linear momentum along the horizontal direction and the mechanical energy of the system are constant during the time interval ti < t < tf. Let's consider a coordinate system with the x-axis horizontal and positive to the right.

Initial State:

  • Ball 1 moving horizontally at speed v.
  • Balls 2 to 5 are at rest.
  • None of the balls are compressing each other (zero elastic potential energy).

The initial momentum of the system along the x-direction:

\(p(t_i)=\;mv\)       (eq. 1)

The initial mechanical energy is only the kinetic of ball 1:

\(E(t_i) = \frac{1}{2}mv^2\)   (eq. 2)

Final State:

  • Balls 4 & 5 moving together horizontally at speed v/2
  • Balls 1 to 3 are at rest.
  • None of the balls are compressing each other (no elastic potential energy).

The final momentum of the system along the x-direction:

\(p(t_f)=m\frac{v}{2}+m\frac{v}{2} = mv\)        (eq. 3)

The final mechanical energy is the kinetic of balls 4 & 5:

\(E(t_f) = \frac{1}{2}m(v/2)^2 + \frac{1}{2}m(v/2)^2 = m\frac{v^2}{4}\)    (eq. 4)

By comparing (eq. 1) and (eq. 3) we find that the linear momentum is constant during the collisions, but by comparing (eq. 2) and (eq. 4) we found that the mechanical energy is not constant. Then the event of balls 4 & 5 moving at speed v/2 after ball 1 hits ball 2 with speed v and stops is not possible.


A meteor approaching a planet [show]
A meteor of mass m is approaching a planet of mass M (M >> m) and radius R. When the meteor is very far away from the planet it has a speed vi. Suppose the meteor just grazes the planet, find the impact parameter h, which is the perpendicular distance between the initial meteor's trajectory and the center of the planet as indicated in the figure.

Solution: [show]
System: [SHOW System]

The meteor treated as a point particle and the planet treated as an infinitely massive object.


Interactions: [SHOW Interactions]
  • External:

We neglect the external influence of other celestial objects.

  • Internal:

Meteor ↔ Planet. The gravitational forces between the two objects.

There are no external forces and the internal forces are conservative, then the mechanical energy of the meteor-planet system is constant.

In addition, there is no external torque, therefore the angular momentum of the system with respect to any point in space is constant. We will pick the point to be the center of mass of the planet ( cm).


Models: [SHOW Models]

Constant Mechanical energy and constant angular momentum about the center of mass of the planet. Both applied to the meteor planet system.


Approach: [SHOW]

We will consider the time interval, ti < t < tf, that it takes the meteor to move from a distance far away until it grazes the planet.

The initial and final states are summarized in the figures below. (We will assume that the meteor is moving in the xy-plane as shown in the figure).

ti: Instant right before the meteor starts to feel the gravitational force of the planet. tf: Instant right when the meteor is moving tangent to the planet's surface.
\(\vec{r}(t_i)\): meteor's initial position measured from the planet's cm. \(\vec{r}(t_f)\): meteor's final position measured from the planet's cm.
\(\vec{v}_i\): meteor's initial velocity. \(\vec{v}_f\): meteor's final velocity.

Apply the models:

Constant Angular Momentum:
The angular momentum about the planet's center of mass of the meteor-planet system is constant:

\(hmv_i = \;Rmv_f\)     (eq. 1)

Show derivation of (eq. 1) [show]
The total angular momentum of the meteor-planet system is given by the sum of the angular momentum of the meteor and the planet.


\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{sys}=\vec{L}_{cm}^{meteor}+\vec{L}_{cm}^{planet}\)


Planet's angular momentum:

The planet can be translating and rotating. Because we are calculating the angular momentum about the planet's center of mass, then the planet's angular momentum only has a spin component given by:


\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{planet}=I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)


Noting that the only force acting on the planet is the gravitational force applied at its center of mass, then there is no external torque acting on the planet and its angular velocity \(\vec{\omega}\) will not change during the time interval ti < t < tf.


\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{planet}(t_i)=\vec{L}_{cm}^{planet}(t_f)\)


Meteor's angular momentum:

The meteor is assumed to be a point particle then we can neglect any possible spin and its angular momentum only has an orbital component given by:


At the initial time, t = ti,

\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{meteor}(t_i)=\vec{r}(t_i)\times m\vec{v}_i = -hmv_i\hat{k}\)

where we have used that initially the meteor is moving along a straight line located at a distance h from the planet's center of mass.


At the final time t = tf,

\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{meteor}(t_f)=\vec{r}(t_f)\times m\vec{v}_f=-Rmv_f\hat{k}\)

where we have used that the meteor is moving tangent to the planet's surface at the instant when it grazes the planet.


System's angular momentum:

The angular momentum of the system about the planet's center of mass is constant during the approach of the meteor:

\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{sys}(t_i) =\vec{L}_{cm}^{sys}(t_f)\)

implies:

\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{meteor}(t_i)+\vec{L}_{cm}^{planet}(t_i) = \vec{L}_{cm}^{meteor}(t_f) +\vec{L}_{cm}^{planet}(t_f)\)

After canceling the planet's angular momentum from each side of the above equation we obtain:

\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{meteor}(t_i) = \vec{L}_{cm}^{meteor}(t_f)\)

Then the constant angular momentum equation becomes:

\( mhv_i = \; mRv_f \)     (eq. 1)

Constant Mechanical Energy:
The mechanical energy of the meteor-planet is constant:

\(\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2=\frac{1}{2}mv_f^2-G\frac{mM}{R}\)   (eq. 2)

Show derivation of (eq. 2) [show]
The planet can be translating with a speed Vcm and spinning about its axis with angular speed \(\omega \;\). As a result the planet's kinetic energy will have a translational and a rotational term given by:

\(K^{planet}=\frac{1}{2}MV_{cm}^2+\frac{1}{2}I_{cm}^{planet}\omega^2\)

We are assuming that the planet has a very large mass then its motion is not affected by the presence of the meteor. Its center of mass will be moving at a constant speed. In addition, no net torque about the planet's center of mass is acting on it, then the planet's angular speed does not change. As a result, the planet's kinetic energy (translational + rotational) remains constant while the meteor is approaching it.

The meteor is assumed to be a point particle then its kinetic energy is only translational. Considering the gravitational potential energy of the planet-meteor system to be zero at infinity (initial state of the system when the meteor is far away), then

\(E(t_i) =\; E(t_f)\)

implies,

\(\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2=\frac{1}{2}mv_f^2-G\frac{mM}{R}\)   (eq. 2)

where the planet's kinetic energy appearing in both sides of the equation has been canceled because it is constant while the meteor is approaching the planet. The term in the left hand side and the first term in the right hand side are the initial and final kinetic energy of the meteor, respectively.

Note: This is an example of one step problem solved by applying two models simultaneously.


Using (eq. 1) we solve for vf to obtain:

\( v_f=v_i\frac{h}{R}\)

Replacing the above expression in (eq. 2) we obtain:

\(v_i^2=v_i^2(\frac{h}{R})^2-2G\frac{M}{R}\)

Solving for h we obtain:

\(h=\sqrt{R^2+\frac{2GMR}{v_i^2}} \)

A ball hits a rod [show]
A ball of mass m and speed vi is moving on a horizontal and frictionless surface along the +x-axis. The ball hits the end of a rod that is at rest on the surface as shown. The figure is a top view of the situation described (gravity is into the screen). The rod is uniform, has a mass m and a length d. After the collision, the ball continues moving along the +x axis with 80% of its initial speed.

Is there any energy loss during the collision? If so, calculate the loss.

Solution: [show]
To answer the question we need to compare the energy available before the collision (kinetic energy of the ball) with the energy after the collision (kinetic energy of the ball plus the kinetic energy -translational + rotational - of the rod).


System: [SHOW System]

The ball treated as a point particle and the rod treated as a rigid object.


Interactions: [SHOW Interactions]
  • Internal:

ball ↔ rod. The resulting forces are the unknown collision forces. These forces will not change the linear and the angular momentum of the system.

The internal collision forces might not be conservative. In this case, they will do non-conservative work changing the mechanical energy of the system.

  • External:

ball & rod ↔ Earth. The resulting force is the weight on ball & rod.

ball & rod ↔ Surface. Because there is no friction, the resulting contact force is the normal force exerted on the ball & rod by the surface.

1. The external forces are perpendicular to the displacement of the objects so they do not do work.

2. The net external force on the ball-rod system is zero, then the linear momentum of the system is constant.

3. In addition , the net external torque on the system is constant with respect to any point in space, therefore the angular momentum of the system is constant with respect to any point. We will consider the point to be the rod's center of mass, point Q in the figure.


Note: Do not confuse the center of mass of the rod with the center of mass of the system

In order to avoid confusion between the center of mass of the ball-rod system and the center of mass of the rod, we use the point Q when we refer to the center of mass of the rod and vrod when we refer to the speed of the rod's center of mass.


Models: [SHOW Models]
  • Constant Linear Momentum
  • Constant Angular Momentum about the center of mass of the rod.
  • Mechanical Energy, External Work, and Internal-Non Conservative work.

The three models are applied to the ball-rod system.


Approach: [SHOW]

We will consider the collision to last a time interval defined as: ti < t < tf.

The initial and final states are summarized in the figures below.

ti: Instant right before the ball hits the rod. tf: Instant right after the ball looses contact with the rod.
The ball is moving to right with speed vi The ball is moving to the right with speed vf = 0.8vi
The rod is at rest. The rod is translating with speed vrod and rotating clockwise with angular speed ω

We are describing the motion of the objects with respect to a coordinate system fixed at the surface. Its +x-axis is parallel to the ball's direction of motion, and its y axis is along the axis of the rod. When the rod is at rest, its center of mass coincides with origin of the coordinate system.

Apply the models:

Constant Linear Momentum:
The linear momentum of the ball-rod system is constant:


\(mv_i\hat{i}=m\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{i}+m\vec{v}_{rod}\)    (eq. 1)

Show derivation of (eq. 1): [show]
The initial momentum:

Only the ball is moving, then the initial linear momentum of the system is the one of the ball:

\(\vec{P}^{sys}(t_i)=mv_i\hat{i}\)


The final momentum: Right after the collision, the ball and the rod are moving. The momentum of the system is the sum of momentum of the ball and the rod:

\(\vec{P}^{sis}(t_f)=mv_f\hat{i}+\vec{v}_{rod}= m\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{i}+\vec{v}_{rod}\)


The linear momentum is constant, implies:

\(mv_i\hat{i}=m\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{i}+m\vec{v}_{rod}\)

Constant Angular Momentum about point Q:
The angular momentum of the system about point Q is constant implies:


\(-m\frac{d}{2}v_i\;\hat{k}=-m\frac{d}{2}\mbox{0.8}v_i\;\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\;\hat{k}\)    (eq. 2)

where \(I_Q\;\) is the moment of inertia of the rod about its center of mass.

Show derivation of (eq. 2): [show]
The initial angular momentum about point Q: Only the ball is moving, then the initial angular momentum is that of one point particle moving along a straight line:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{sys}(t_i)=-m\frac{d}{2}v_i\hat{k}\)

where \(+\hat{k}\) is pointing out of the screen.


The final angular momentum about point Q: Right after the collision, the angular momentum of the system is the sum of the angular momentum of the ball (point particle moving along a straight line) and the angular momentum of a rigid object rotating about an axis passing through its center of mass:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{sys}(t_f)=-m\frac{d}{2}v_f\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\hat{k}\)

where IQ, is the moment of inertia of a uniform rod about its center of mass.

Using that the speed after the collision is 80% of the speed before the collision, the final angular momentum becomes:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{sys}(t_f)=-m\frac{d}{2}\mbox{0.8}v_i\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\hat{k}\)

Note: because we have chosen to calculate the angular momentum with respect to point Q, the rod's center of mass, then the rod's angular momentum only has the spin component.


The angular momentum of the system about point Q is constant implies:


\(-m\frac{d}{2}v_i\;\hat{k}=-m\frac{d}{2}\mbox{0.8}v_i\;\hat{k}-I_Q\;\omega\;\hat{k}\)

From (eq. 1) we can solve for the velocity of the rod's center of mass:


\(\vec{v}_{rod}=\mbox{0.2}v_i\;\hat{i}\)      (eq. 3)


Using (eq. 2) we obtain the angular speed of the rod:


\(\omega=\frac{md\;\mbox{0.2}\;v_i}{2I_Q}\)

Inserting the value of the moment of inertia of a rod about its center of mass, \(I_Q=\;md^2/12\), in the above equation we obtain:


\(\omega=\mbox{1.2}\frac{v_i}{d}\)    (eq. 4)


Mechanical Energy:

The mechanical energy of the system is only kinetic.


Initial Mechanical energy:

Only the ball is moving:

\(E(t_i)=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2\)   (eq. 5)


Final Mechanical energy:

The ball and the rod are moving:

\(E(t_f)\;=\;K^{ball}(t_f)+K^{rod}(t_F)\)


The rod is a rigid object rotating and translating then its kinetic energy has a translational and rotational component:


\(E(t_f)\;=\Big(\frac{1}{2}m(0.8v_i)^2\Big)+\Big(\frac{1}{2}mv_{rod}^2+\frac{1}{2}I_Q\;\omega^2\Big)\)


Using (eq. 3) and (eq. 4) and the value of the moment of inertia of a rod about its center of mass, the final mechanical energy is given by:

\(E(t_f)\;=\frac{1}{2}m(0.8v_i)^2+\frac{1}{2}m(0.2v_i)^2+\frac{1}{2}(\frac{md^2}{12})(\frac{1.2v_i}{d})^2\)


rearranging terms,


\(E(t_f)\;=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2(0.8^2+0.2^2+\frac{1.2^2}{12})\)

\(E(t_f)\;=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2(0.64+0.04+0.12)=\frac{1}{2}mv_i^2\;0.8\)

The energy decreased by 20%:

\((E(t_f)-E(t_i))/E(t_i)\;=\;(0.8-1)=-0.2\)
