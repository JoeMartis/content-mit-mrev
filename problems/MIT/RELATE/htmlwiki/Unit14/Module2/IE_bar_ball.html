Illustrative Example: Knocking down a bar with a ball
A ball of mass m is thrown horizontally with a speed vo. It hits a thin and uniform bar of total mass M and length D. The bar is initially at rest and vertical. It is connected to the ground through a hinge at its bottom end as shown in the left figure below. The ball hits the bar at a distance d measured from the upper end. The impact is such, that the ball stops moving right after hitting the bar. What is the bar's angular speed right before it reaches the ground? Assume that there is no friction between the bar and the hinge.
How many steps does the problem have?

(Use a short sentence to describe the event occurring in each step.)

[show]
Answer: 2 steps


Step 1: [show description]
The collision of the ball and the bar

Step 2: [show description]
The bar falls down rotating about the hinge an angle of 90o.

Note: Very likely your sentences do not coincide exactly with those shown above but they describe the same event.

1. Chose the system for each step [show]
Step 1: ball + bar. The ball is treated as a point particle and the bar as a rigid object. Why this system?[show answer]
Because the contact forces between the ball and the bar are unknown. If these two objects formed the system then these unknown collision forces become internal. What is the advantage of having internal forces?

Steps 2: the bar (rigid object) and the Earth (point particle of infinite mass).Note:[show]

If the Earth is assumed to be a massive object, then its motion is negligible while the bar is falling.

3. 4. & 5. Describe the interactions, select and apply the model on each step. [show]
Step 1: ball-bar collision

System: Ball & Bar

Interactions: [show]
  • Internal: The ball-bar interaction. The resulting forces are the unknown collision forces of magnitudes FMm and FmM in the free body diagrams. These forces are Newton's 3rd law pairs.
  • External:
    • Ball ↔ Earth: gravity on the ball (mg).
    • Bar ↔ Earth: gravity on the bar (Mg).
    • Bar ↔ hinge: contact force exerted on the bar by the hinge (FMH). This force is applied at the bottom end of the bar, then its torque calculated with respect to the hinge is zero (because the moment arm is zero). The hinge is referred to H in the free body diagram below.


Note: This force has a horizontal component that can't be neglected, therefore the linear momentum of the system is not constant during the collision.


Model: [show]
Constant Angular Momentum

Apply the model: [show]

Initial Angular Momentum with respect to the hinge:

\(\vec{L}_H(t_i)= -mv_o(D-d)\hat{k}\)

Final Angular Momentum with respect to the hinge:

\(\vec{L}_H(t_1) = -I_H\omega_1\hat{k}\)

where IH = MD2/3 is the moment of inertia of the rod about the hinge, and \(\hat{k}\) is the unit vector along the z-axis that is pointing out of the screen.

The change of angular momentum about the hinge is:

\( \vec{L}(t_1) = \vec{L}(t_i)+\int_{t_i}^{t_1}\sum_{ext}\vec{\tau}_H\; dt\)

In the short time collision approximation the sum of the external torques about the hinge is zero:

Explanation of the zero net external torque: [show]
The sum of the external torques about the hinge is the sum of the torques due to gravity on the bar and on the ball plus the torque due to the force exerted on the bar by the hinge (FMH):


\(\sum_{ext}\vec{\tau}_H=\vec{\tau}_H^{mg} + \vec{\tau}_H^{Mg}+\vec{\tau}_H^{F_{MH}}\)

  • The last term is zero because the force FMH is being applied at the hinge.
  • If we assume a short time collision, then the bar remains vertical during the collision. As a result the bar's weight and the position vector of the bar's center of mass are along the same line and their cross product is zero.
  • During the short time that the collision lasts, the ball does not move horizontally, then its weigh and the position vector of the ball measured from the hinge are also along the same line, therefore the cross product is zero.

As a result the angular momentum is constant:

\( \vec{L}(t_1) = \vec{L}(t_i)\)


\(-mv_o\;(D-d)= -I_H\omega_1\;\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 1)}\)


Step 2: The bar falling down.
 System: Bar & Earth

Interactions: [show]
  • Internal: The bar and the Earth. It is a conservative interaction represented by the gravitational potential energy.
  • External: The contact force between the hinge and the bar. It is assumed that there is no friction, then this force does not do work on the bar while it is falling.


Model: [show]
Constant Mechanical Energy

Apply the model: [show]

We will consider the zero of potential energy at the ground.

Initial Mechanical Energy

\(E(t_1) = \frac{1}{2}I_H\omega_1^2+Mg\frac{D}{2}\)

Final Mechanical Energy

\(E(t_f)= \frac{1}{2}I_H\omega_f^2\)

The mechanical energy is constant:

\( E(t_f) = \; E(t_1) \)

\(\frac{1}{2}I_H\omega_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}I_H\omega_1^2+Mg\frac{D}{2} \;\;\;\ \mbox{(eq. 2)}\)


Put the steps together to obtain the solution [show]
Step 1: Constant angular momentum applied to the ball+bar system:

\(-mv_o\;(D-d)= -I_H\omega_1\;\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 1)}\)

Step 2: Constant mechanical energy applied to the bar+Earth system:

\(\frac{1}{2}I_H\omega_f^2 = \frac{1}{2}I_H\omega_1^2+Mg\frac{D}{2} \;\;\;\ \mbox{(eq. 2)}\)

From (eq, 2) the angular speed right before the bar hits the ground is:

\(\omega_f = \sqrt{\omega_1^2+\frac{MgD}{I_H} }\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 3)}\)

From (eq. 1) we obtain the initial angular speed:

\(\omega_1 = \frac{mv_o\;(D-d)}{I_H} \;\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 4)}\)

Replacing (eq. 4) into (eq. 3) and using the expression of IH = MD2/3 we obtain:

\(\omega_f = \sqrt{\bigg( 3\frac{m}{M}v_o\frac{(D-d)}{D^2}\bigg)^2 + 3\frac{g}{D}}\)
