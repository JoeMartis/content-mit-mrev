Illustrative Example: A bouncing ball

A ball of mass m is released from rest from a height hi, it hits the surface of a

horizontal table and it bounces back vertically upwards in such a way that the maximum height reached by the ball is hf (hf < hi ). If the ball is in contact with table during a short time interval T, what is the average force exerted on the ball by the table? (Review the definition of AVERAGE FORCE)
)
Definition of average force:

If a force depends on time, its average value over a time interval t1 < t < t2 is given by:

\(\vec{f}_{avg}=\frac{1}{t_2-t_1}\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\vec{f}(t) \; dt\)



Solution

How many steps does the problem have?

Answer: 3 steps


Step 1: [show description]
The ball is moving down

 Step 2: [show description]
Collision of the ball and the table

 Step 3: [show description]
The ball is moving up

Note: Very likely your sentences do not coincide exactly with those shown above but they describe the same event.

1. Chose the system for each step [show]
Step 1 & 3: ball treated as a point particle and the Earth treated as a particle of infinite mass. Note:[show]
If the Earth is assumed to be a massive object, then its motion is negligible while the ball is moving.

Steps 2: the ball (treated as a point particle).

2. Define the initial and final states of each of the steps [show]
The time interval under consideration, ti < t < tf , is divided in three sub-intervals as shown in the figure:

where t1 and t2 are the times corresponding to the intermediate states.

We will define a coordinate system with the +y - axis vertically up and with the zero coinciding with the table's surface.

Step 1: Moving down
Initial State of step 1 Final State of step 1
ti: instant right when the ball is released t1 : instant right before the ball hits the table
The ball is at rest and its height is hi The ball is moving down with speed v1 and its height is 0
Step 2: Collision

t1 : instant right before the ball hits the table t2 : instant right after the ball looses contact with the table

Step 3: Moving up
Initial State of step 3 Final State of step 3
t2 : instant right after the ball looses contact with the table tf : instant right when the ball stops momentarily in the air
The ball is moving up with speed v2 and its height is zero. The ball is at rest and its height is hf.
3. 4. & 5. Describe the interactions, select and apply the model on each step. [show]
Step 1:moving down

System: Ball & Earth

Interactions: [show]
  • Internal: The ball and the Earth. It is a conservative interaction represented by the gravitational potential energy.
  • External: none.
Model: [show]
Constant Mechanical Energy

Apply the model: [show]
Initial Mechanical Energy Final Mechanical Energy
\(E(t_i) = mg\;h_i\) \(E(t_1)= 1/2 mv_1^2\)

\( E(t_1)\;=\;E(t_i)\)
\( \frac{1}{2} mv_1^2 = mg\; h_i\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 1)}\)


Step 2:collision
 System: the ball

Interactions: [show]

There are only external interactions:

1. The ball and the Earth: gravity on the ball.

2.The ball and the table: the contact force exerted on the ball by the table. This is the unknown force and we will call it: \(\vec{f}(t)\)

The net external force is \(\sum_{ext}\vec{F}=f(t)\hat{j}-mg\hat{j}\)



Model: [show]
Momentum and External Impulse

Apply the model: [show]
Initial Momentum Final Momentum
\(\vec{p}(t_1) = -mv_1\; \hat{j}\) \(\vec{p}(t_2)= +mv_2\; \hat{j}\)

\( \vec{p}(t_2)=\vec{p}(t_1)+\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\sum_{ext}\vec{F} dt\)

If we consider the collision to last a short time interval, T = t2 - t1 << 1, then the impulse due to the gravitational force can be neglected and the change of linear momentum is given by: \( \vec{p}(t_2)=\vec{p}(t_1)+\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\vec{f}dt\)

Then, the average force exerted on the ball by the table is: \(\vec{f}_{avg}=\frac{1}{T}\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\vec{f}dt = \frac{\vec{p}(t_2)-\vec{p}(t_1)}{T}\)

or

\(\vec{f}_{avg}=\frac{mv_2-(-mv_1)}{T}\hat{j}=m\frac{v_2+v_1}{T}\hat{j} \;\;\;\; \mbox{ (eq. 2)}\)


Step 3:moving up
 System: Ball & Earth

Interactions: [show]
  • Internal: The ball and the Earth, conservative interaction represented by the gravitational potential energy.
  • External: none.

Model: [show]
Constant Mechanical Energy

Apply the model: [show]
Initial Mechanical Energy Final Mechanical Energy
\(E(t_2) =\frac{1}{2} mv_2^2\) \(E(t_f)= mg\;h_f\)

\( E(t_f)\; = \; E(t_2)\)

\( mg\;h_f = \frac{1}{2} mv_2^2 \;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 3)}\)

Put the steps together to obtain the solution [show]
Step 1: Constant mechanical energy applied to the ball+Earth system

\( \frac{1}{2} mv_1^2 = mg\; h_i\;\;\;\;\;\;\; \mbox{ (eq. 1)}\)

Step 2: Momentum and external impulse applied to the ball (assuming a short time collision)

\(\vec{f}_{avg}= m\frac{v_2+v_1}{T}\hat{j} \;\;\;\; \mbox{ (eq. 2)}\)

Step 3: Constant mechanical energy applied to the ball+Earth system.

\( mg\;h_f = \frac{1}{2} mv_2^2 \;\;\;\;\;\;\; \mbox{(eq. 3)}\)

From (eq. 1) and (eq. 3):

\(v_1 =\sqrt{2gh_i} \mbox{, } v_2=\sqrt{2gh_f}\)


Replacing in (eq. 2) we have that the average force exerted on the ball by the table is:


\(\vec{f}_{avg}= m\frac{(\sqrt{2gh_f}+\sqrt{2gh_i})}{T}\;\hat{j}\)


Note: The same answer is obtained if the solution to the sub-problems in steps 1 and 2 is obtained using 1-D kinematics models.
