Adding Force Vectors by Decomposing into Components

Recall that Newton's Second Law is expressed as a vector equation: \( \vec{F}_{net} = m\vec{a}\) . Using vector decomposition in the Cartesian (x-y-z) coordinate system, we can rewrite the vector equation as three scalar equations, one for each component (each direction in space):

 

  • \( F_{net,x} = \sum F_{x} = ma_{x} \)

and

  • \( F_{net,y} = \sum F_{y} = ma_{y} \)

and

  • \( F_{net,z} = \sum F_{z} = ma_{z} \)

Note that all three equations have the same form. If all of our vectors lie in a plane (i.e., are two dimensional), we would only need the first two equations.

To add vectors using components: first, decompose each vector into scalar components, add the components, and finally reassemble the resultant vector from the summed components.

Remember, convenience is in the eye of the problem solver, so here are some criteria to consider that simplify the component equations

  1. align one axis with the direction of the acceleration, IF you know the acceleration direction. This generates at least one or two equations that equal zero, since the acceleration in one or both of the other directions must be zero with this choice.
  2. align the most forces with coordinate directions. For example, if there are three forces aligned with one or more of the coordinate directions and less than three in any other directions, then a coordinate system aligned with those three forces creates force components equal to zero for all of those forces.
  1. Sum the corresponding components, paying careful attention to the direction (sign) of each component.
  2. Use the resultant mutually perpendicular component sums to form the resultant vector.
  3. Use those perpendicular components to draw the resultant vector.

To re-express a vector that is given by components in terms of magnitude and angle 

  • Use the Pythagorean theorem to determine the magnitude of the resultant vector and trigonometry to determine the direction with respect to the chosen coordinate system.
