Adding Force Vectors Geometrically

Think of vectors as arrows that can be moved around freely but not rotated (remember: both size and direction matter for vector quantities). Add two vectors by putting the tail-end of one vector on the arrow-end (called the head) of the other as is shown in the two diagrams. It does not matter which vector's tail is placed on the arrow of the other - the sum is the same (called the commutative property). 

In fact we can keep adding more and more vectors using this head-to-tail method. Geometric addition is very helpful in sketching out preliminary predictions in problems, for example the direction of Fnet (a vector sum of forces) will always point in the same direction as the acceleration vector. You can draw any vector anywhere on the page, pointed in the correct direction and scaled appropriately, so that the length drawn on your paper corresponds to the magnitude.

  1. Choose an appropriate scale for length.
  2. Draw one of the vectors in the sum to scale, in the correct direction.
  3. Draw the next vector in the sum with the tail of 2 at the head of the first.
  4. Keep drawing in new vectors with their tails at the head of the preceding vector until all the vectors in the sum are drawn.
  5. Finally, draw the vector that starts at the first tail and ends on the final head, put an arrow on it and measure its length according to the scale. This is called the resultant vector (or "net vector of the sum").
