Newton's First Law

Before we describe the effects of interactions, we have to understand what motion to expect in the absence of interactions.

Learning Goals

After completing this module, you should be able to:

  • Describe the motion of an object that experiences no interactions, sometimes called the "natural state of motion".

Statement of the Law

Newton's statement of his first law of motion is [Cajori's translation]:

Every body continues in its state of rest, or of uniform motion in a right line, unless it is compelled to change that state by forces impressed upon it.

Thus, according to Newton, the natural states of translational motion are:

  • motion with constant velocity - i.e. in a straight line path at constant speed
  • rest (no motion) - an important special case of constant velocity (v = 0)

Newton's First Law says when an object experiences no interaction with any other object, then if it is at rest it will remain at rest and if in motion it will remain in motion along a straight line path with constant speed. Previous theories of motion (Aristotle, Impetus Theory), held that the natural state of motion was at rest, in common with everyday observation.

Inertia

Newton's first law implies the concept of Inertia, the tendency of matter to maintain its state of rest or motion with constant velocity.


Newton's Laws of Motion refer to the causes of any change in the motion of an object. Translational or linear motion of an object is evident by any change in the object's position along a straight line path. We will discuss translational motion in the first part of this course, and rotational motion later in the course. When analyzing translational motion, interactions are manifested as forces acting on the bodies participating in the interaction.
