Illustrative Example: Meteor Approaching a Planet

A small meteor of mass m is moving along a straight line (in free space) with a speed Vo when it enters the gravitational field region of a planet of mass M and radius R as shown in the figure. The distance between the straight line and the center of the planet (point Q) is h.

Assume that the meteor is very small that can be treated as a point particle, and that its mass m << M, so that the motion of the gravitational force of the meteor on the planet does not change the planet's motion.

Consider te motion of the meteor between the time interval ti<t<tf, where, ti, is the instant at which the meteor is far away from the planet and moving moving with Vo, and tf, is the instant at which the meteor just grazes the planet (the meteor does not collide with the planet).

Question 1: Which of the following statements is true about the angular momentum of the meteor about point Q':

1. \(\vec{L}_Q(t_f) > \vec{L}_Q(t_i)\)

2. \(\vec{L}_Q(t_f) = \vec{L}_Q(t_i)\)

3. \(\vec{L}_Q(t_f) < \vec{L}_Q(t_i)\)

Answer [show]
Option 2. The angular momentum is constant
Explanation [show]
Consider the system to be the meteor and is treated as a point particle. It only interacts with the planet through the gravitational interaction. At any point along its trajectory, the gravitational force exerted on the meteor by the planet, \(\vec{F}_{mM}\), is pointing towards the center of the planet, point Q.

Therefore, the torque about point Q:

\[\vec{\tau}_Q=\vec{r}_Q\times\vec{F}_{mM}=0\]

The position vector from point Q to the point of application of the force \(\vec{r}_Q\) is at 180o angle with respect to \(\vec{F}_{mM}\)

If the net torque is zero, then the meteor's angular momentum about point Q is constant
