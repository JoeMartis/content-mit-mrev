Illustrative Example: The Earth in Orbit

The Earth of mass m and radius a moves with a speed vcmalong an elliptic orbit around the sun. The Earth rotates with speed ω about an axis that passes trough its center of mass. As shown in the figure, the axis of rotation is at angle with respect to the plane on which the Earth's path is contained. At the instant shown in the figure, the distance between the center of the Sun and the center of the Earth is R.

What is the angular momentum vector with respect to the center of the Sun, point S, at the instant shown in the figure? Express your answer in terms of some or all of the variables R,a, vcm, ω and the unit vectors shown in the figure.

Answer [show]
\(\vec{L}_{s} = Rmv_{cm}\hat{k} + \frac{2}{5}ma^2\omega\hat{k'}\)
Solution [show]
The Earth is rotating and translating therefore the angular momentum vector with respect to the center of mass of the Sun is the sum of the orbital and the spin components:

\(\vec{L}_s =\vec{r}_{s,cm} \times m\vec{v}_{cm} + I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)

where Icm is the moment of inertia of a solid sphere of radius a which is given by (2/5)ma2. In this problem we are assuming that the Earth is a perfect sphere which mass is uniformly distributed in volume, and \(\vec{r}_{s,cm}\) of magnitude R is the position vector of the Earth's center of mass measured with respect to the center of the Sun (point S).

Orbital:

This component represents the angular momentum about point S of a point particle of mass m moving with speed vcm. At the instant shown in the figure the position and velocity vectors are perpendicular to each other:

\(\vec{r}_{s,cm}\perp\vec{v}_{cm}\)

then the magnitude of the orbital component is:

\(|\vec{L}_{s}^{orbital}|=Rmv_{cm}\)

Following the right hand rule, the direction of the orbital component is perpendicular to the plane defined by the Earth's orbit and pointing towards the +z axis:

\(\vec{L}_{s}^{orbital} = Rmv_{cm}\hat{k}\)


Spin:

The spin component is due to the motion of all the particles rotating about the axis passing through the Earth's center of mass. In the figure, the direction of this axis is the direction of the unit vector \(\hat{k'}\), which is at an angle to the unit vector \(\hat{k}\). Then the spin component becomes:

\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{spin}=\frac{2}{5}ma^2\omega\hat{k'}\)
