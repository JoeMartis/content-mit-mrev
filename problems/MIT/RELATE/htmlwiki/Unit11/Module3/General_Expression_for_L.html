The Angular Momentum of a Rigid Object Rotating and Translating

Consider a rigid object of mass m translating with a speed vcm and rotating with angular speed ω about an axis that passes through its center of mass as shown below. The motion of the object is contained in the xy-plane and the axis of rotation is along the z-axis.

Let Q be the point relative to which we locate the position of the center of mass of the object, \(\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\). The angular momentum about point Q is:

\(\vec{L}_Q = \vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times m\vec{v}_{cm} + I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)

where Icm is the moment of inertia of the object about its center of mass.

Decomposing the Motion into Translation and Rotation

The angular momentum is decomposed into two vector parts:

Orbital (translation of the center of mass): Spin (rotation about the axis):
\(\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times m\vec{v}_{cm}\) \(I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)
This represents the angular momentum about point Q of a point particle of mass m translating with speed vcm. This is the angular momentum associated to the rotational motion of the object about an axis passing through its center of mass.

 

Derivation [show]
We will derive the expression of the angular momentum of a rigid object translating and rotating. We will do the derivation for an object consisting of two particles and then generalize the results for a continuous rigid object.

Consider the object shown in the left figure below consisting of two particles of masses m1 and m2 connected by a rod of negligible mass. The object translates with a speed vcm and rotates with angular speed ω about an axis passing through its center of mass and perpendicular to the xy-plane.



The angular momentum of the object about a given point Q is the sum of the angular momentum of the object's constituents. Because the rod has a negligible mass then:

\(\vec{L}_Q = \vec{L}_Q^1 + \vec{L}_Q^2 = \vec{r}_{Q,1} \times m_1\vec{v}_1 + \vec{r}_{Q,2} \times m_2\vec{v}_2\)     (eq.1)

where \(\vec{r}_{Q,1}\) ,  \(\vec{r}_{Q,2}\)  and \(\vec{v}_1\) ,  \(\vec{v}_2\)  are the position and velocity vectors of particles 1 and 2 measured with respect to a coordinate system with origin at point Q (see right figure above). The particles' position vectors can be expressed as:

\(\vec{r}_{Q,1} = \vec{r}_{Q,cm} + \vec{r}_{cm,1} \)        (eq.2)

\(\vec{r}_{Q,2} = \vec{r}_{Q,cm} + \vec{r}_{cm,2} \)        (eq.3)

where,  \(\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\)  is the position of the center of mass measured from point Q and  \(\vec{r}_{cm,1}\) , \(\vec{r}_{cm,2}\)  are the particles' position measured from a coordinate system with origin at the center of mass of the object.

In the figure below, only the position vectors of particle 1 with respect to point Q,  \(\vec{r}_{Q,1}\) , and with respect to the center of mass,  \(\vec{r}_{cm,1}\) , are shown. Included in the figure is the position vector of the center of mass measured from point Q,  \(\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\).

Taking the derivative with respect to time of equations (2) and (3) we obtain that:

\(\vec{v}_{1} = \vec{v}_{cm} + \vec{v}_{cm,1} \)        (eq.4)

\(\vec{v}_{2} = \vec{v}_{cm} + \vec{v}_{cm,2} \)        (eq.5)

where  \(\vec{v}_{cm,1}\) , and  \(\vec{v}_{cm,2}\) are the velocity vectors of the particles with respect to the center of mass.


Using (eqs. 2 to 5) in (eq. 1), the angular momentum of the object about point Q becomes:

\(\vec{L}_Q = \sum_{i=1}^{2}\vec{r}_{Q,i}\times m_i\vec{v}_i = \)

\(=\sum_{i=1}^{2}(\vec{r}_{Q,cm} + \vec{r}_{cm,i})\times m_i (\vec{v}_{cm} + \vec{v}_{cm,i}) \)

where we have used the symbol Σ to indicate the sum of each of the physical quantities (mass, position and velocity) corresponding to the particles 1 and 2. After doing the multiplications we obtain four terms:

\(\vec{L}_Q =\sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times \vec{v}_{cm}+ \sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times\vec{v}_{cm,i} +\)

\( + \sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{cm,i}\times\vec{v}_{cm} + \sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{cm,i}\times\vec{v}_{cm,i} \)          (eq.6)


Note that the vectors  \(\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\)  and \(\vec{r}_{cm}\)  do not depend on the i subscript and they can be taken outside of the sum.

Orbital Component:

The first term in (eq. 6) is the orbital component. By taking the position and velocity vectors of the center of mass outside of the summation the first term becomes:

\(\sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times \vec{v}_{cm} = (\sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i)(\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times \vec{v}_{cm}) = m\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times \vec{v}_{cm}\)

where m is the total mass of the object, m = m1 + m2.

Then, the orbital component of the angular momentum is given by:

\(\vec{L}_Q^{orbital}=\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times m\vec{v}_{cm}\)

This term represents the angular momentum with respect to point Q of a point particle of mass m moving with speed vcm.

Spin Component:

The last term in (eq. 6) is called the spin angular momentum. Note that the quantity inside the sum, \( m_i\vec{r}_{cm,i}\times\vec{v}_{cm,i}\) , represents the angular momentum of particle i about the center of mass. For an observer at the center of mass, the particle i is spinning about the axis passing through the center of mass. Because we are considering that the axis does not change direction during the object's motion then this term is the one that we have already obtained in the case of a fixed axis of rotation in Module 2:

\( \sum_{i=1}^{2} m_i\vec{r}_{cm,i}\times\vec{v}_{cm,i} = I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)

Then, the spin component is:

\(\vec{L}_{cm}^{spin}=I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)

The second and third terms in (eq. 6) are zero:

By the definition of the velocity and the position of the center of mass the second and third terms are zero.

Prove [show]
Second term in (eq. 6):

Taking \(\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\) out of the sum, the second term becomes:

\(\sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times\vec{v}_{cm,i} =\vec{r}_{Q,cm}\sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\times\vec{v}_{cm,i} = 0\).

where \(\sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\times\vec{v}_{cm,i} \) is m times the velocity of the center of mass relative to the center of mass and this is zero by definition.


The third term in (eq.6)

Taking \(\vec{v}_{cm}\) out f the sum, the third term becomes:

\( \sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{cm,i}\times\vec{v}_{cm} = (\sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{cm,i})\times\vec{v}_{cm} = 0 \times \vec{v}_{cm} = 0\)

where we have used that the position of the center of mass

\( \sum_{i=1}^{2}m_i\vec{r}_{cm,i}/(m_1+m_2)\)

measured from the center of mass is zero by definition

Generalizing the result

The expression of the angular momentum about point Q obtained for the case of the 2-particle object:

\(\vec{L}_{Q} = \vec{r}_{Q,cm} \times \vec{v}_{cm} + I_{cm}\vec{\omega}\)

is also valid for an object constituted by N particles. This can be shown by changing the limits in the summation used in the derivation above:

\(\sum_{i=1}^2 \rightarrow \sum_{i=1}^N\)

Similarly, the expression above is valid for a continuous rigid object if the summation in the derivation above is replaced by an integral:

\(\sum_{i=1}^2 \rightarrow \int dm\)
