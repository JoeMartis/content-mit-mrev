Example 3. A particle moving along a straight line

A particle moving with a constant velocity as shown.

Which of the following statements about the angular momentum about point Q are true:

  1. the magnitude is constant but its direction is not
  2. the magnitude and the direction are constant
  3. the direction is constant but the magnitude is not.
Answer [show]
Options 2 is true.

Let's consider two instant of times t = t1 and t = t2 as indicated in the figure above. As shown, the particle's position vector depends on time as well as the angle θ(t) between the position and velocity vectors.

Direction:

For both instants of time, t1 and t2, we follow the right hand rule, to find that the direction of the angular momentum is into the screen, along the -z axis. Then the direction of the angular momentum is constant with time.

Magnitude: From the definition, the magnitude is given by:

LQ = mrQ(t)vsinθ(t)

Both rQ(t) and θ(t) depend on time but the perpendicular distance define as:

rQ(t)sinθ(t) = d is constant,

then the magnitude of the angular is given by:

LQ = dmv which does not depends on time because v does not.

The magnitude is constant.

Example 4. A particle moving on a plane

A particle of mass m = 10 kg is moving on the xy plane. At a given instant of time its position measured from a point S is:

\[\vec{r}_S = 2 m\hat{i} - 3 m\hat{j}\]

and its speed is

\[\vec{v} = 1 m/s\hat{i} + 5 m/s \hat{j}\]

Calculate the particle's angular momentum with respect to point S.

Answer [show]
\(\vec{L}_S=\vec{r}_S\times m\vec{v} \)

\(= 10 kg(2 m\hat{i} - 3 m\hat{j})\times(1 m/s\hat{i} + 5 m/s \hat{j}) \)

\(= 10(2\hat{i}\times\hat{i} + 10 \hat{i}\times\hat{j} -3\hat{j}\times\hat{i}-15\hat{j}\times\hat{j})kg.m^2/s\)

\(= 10(0 + 10\hat{k} +3\hat{k} + 0)kgm^2/s = 130 kg.m^2/s \hat{k} \)
