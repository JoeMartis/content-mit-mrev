Angular Momentum of a Point Particle

Learning Objectives for this Module

At the end of this module you should be able to:

  • Calculate the magnitude and the direction of the angular momentum vector of a point particle about a specified point.
  • Explain why the angular momentum vector depends on the point used to calculate its value.

 

Motivation of the concept

The angular momentum of a point particle is a measure of the "circulation" of the particle's linear momentum about some specific point. To get a feel for the concept of angular momentum, imagine that you are standing at the center of a frictionless turntable and facing toward -x axis. In the figure below a sketch of your arms and hands are shown. We call the center of the turntable point Q.

The turntable is initially not rotating. Three balls, A, B, and C are traveling in the positive x direction toward you, all with the same mass m and the same speed v, and hence with the same linear momentum.

\[ \vec{p} = m v \hat{x}\]


If you catch ball A (with your right hand), the ball's linear momentum is transferred to you. As a result, your right hand will recoil and you will start to rotate clockwise (when viewed from above). Similarly, if you catch ball C (with your left hand) your left hand will recoil and you will start to rotate counter-clockwise. The effect of the linear momentum transfer on the rotation depends on how far is the ball from the center of rotation when you catch it. The shorter the extension of your arms smaller is the resulting rotation. If you catch ball B (with both hands) you will not rotate at all.

Note that the direction of the velocity the ball has before hitting your hand is also important. If the ball hits your right hand at an angle with respect to your stretched arm only the component of the linear momentum perpendicular to the arm will contribute to the resulting rotation.


The resulting rotation due to the transfer of linear momentum during the collision will thus depend on both the ball's position with respect to the center of rotation, point Q, and the angle between the ball's position and the ball's linear momentum.
