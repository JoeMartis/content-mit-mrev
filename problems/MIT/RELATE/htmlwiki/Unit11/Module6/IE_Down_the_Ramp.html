Illustrative Example: Down The Ramp

Consider a ball placed on a ramp inclined at an angle θ above the horizontal.

What is the acceleration of the ball's center of mass as it rolls down the ramp? Assume that the ball rolls without slipping.

System: [show]
The ball is treated as a rigid body.
Interactions: [show]
External influences from the earth (gravity) and the ramp (friction and normal force).
Model: [show]
Angular Momentum and Angular Impulse
Diagramatic Representation [show]
The ball will translate and rotate as it rolls down the slope. The relevant free body diagram is shown below.

Mathematical Representation [show]
Since the friction force is unknown, it is best to choose a point on the surface of the ramp such as the the point Q shown in the picture below.

The torques about this point will depend upon the position of the ball. When the ball has moved a distance x along the ramp,

  • the torque from the normal is out of the screen, along the +z-axis and its magnitude:

\[\tau_Q^{N} = Nx \]

  • the torque from gravity is into the screen directed along the -z - axis and its magnitude is:

\( \tau_Q^{mg} = mg(x\cos\theta +R\sin\theta) \)

The expression above is most easealy found using the lever arm:

The net torque is then:

\[\vec{\tau}_Q^{mg} = -mg(x\cos\theta+R\sin\theta) +Nx)\hat{k} \]

Because there is no motion perpendicular to the incline surface, then

\[\sum F_y = 0 \rightarrow N = mg\cos\theta \]

Replacing this value in the expression of the net torque we obtain:

\[ \sum\vec{\tau}_Q = -mgR \sin \theta\hat{k}\]

We must now find an expression for the angular momentum of the ball. Because the ball is translating and rotating about its center of mass, the ball's angular momentum is composed of the orbital and the spin components:

\[\vec{L}_Q = \vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times m \vec{v}_{cm} + I_{cm}\vec\omega\]

Using the right hand rule both components, the orbital (\( \vec{r}_{Q,cm}\times m \vec{v}_{cm}\)) and the spin (\(I_{cm}\vec\omega\)) are into the screen, along the -z-axis. The above expression becomes:

\[\vec{L}_Q = -(mRv_{cm} + I_{cm}\omega)\hat{k}\]

We can relate vcm and ω using the rolling without slipping constraint: ωcmR = vcm

Substituting gives:

\[\vec{L}_Q = -(mRv_{cm} +I_{\rm cm} \frac{v_{\rm cm}}{R})\hat{k}=-(m+\frac{I_{cm}}{R^2})v_{cm}R\hat{k}\]

Taking the time derivative of the above expression and set it to the magnitude of the sum of the torques we obtain:

\[-(m+\frac{I_{cm}}{R^2})a_{cm}R\hat{k} = - mgR\sin\theta \hat{k}\]

so that we find:

\[a_{cm} = \frac{g\sin\theta}{1 + \frac{\displaystyle I}{\displaystyle mR^{2}}} \]
