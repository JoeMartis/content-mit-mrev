Angular Momentum

Galaxies, solar systems, figure skaters, atoms, and subatomic particles: all are characterized and governed by angular momentum, the topic of this unit. On the macroscopic level, angular momentum can be thought of as the amount of linear momentum going around a specified point. Angular momentum is an important physical quantity that governs the dynamics of individual rigid bodies and collections of many rigid bodies like the Earth-Moon system or a spiral galaxy.

One reason that the angular momentum of a system is a useful quantity is that internal interactions do not change it - hence it remains constant (conserved) if no external torques act. This is a more general condition (i.e. less strict) than having no external forces act. In particular, a central force - e.g., the gravitational force from the sun - generates no torques on the planets if one takes the sun itself as the reference point. Thus the angular momentum of each of the planet is constant as it orbits the sun, giving rise to the "equal areas in equal times" law of planetary orbits. Even taking into account the (internal) interactions between the planets, the system of all planets still has constant angular momentum about the sun.

Plan of the Angular Momentum Unit

In this unit, we first consider the angular momentum of a point particle relative to an arbitrary axis. Then we give the law describing how angular momentum changes with time. This law applies to systems containing many particles, and we show the connection with the previous unit on angular dynamics (of a rigid body). This allows us to generalize the concept of angular momentum to a spinning rigid body that is moving with respect to the selected reference point. We then consider systems composed of several rigid bodies, especially cases where no external torques act on the combined system (even though there may be a collision inside the system).
