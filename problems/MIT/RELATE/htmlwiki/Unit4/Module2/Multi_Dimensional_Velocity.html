Multi-dimensional Velocity

To describe velocity in a general direction that is not aligned with your coordinate x-axis, you must use vector components. Essentially, we add another copy of the model for each direction that is needed. Thus, for a two dimensional motion in the xy-plane the velocity vector is \(\vec{v}=v_x\hat{i}+v_y\hat{j}\) and we would use two equations simultaneously:

\(\mathcal{}x(t) = x_{i} + v_{x}(t-t_{i})\)

\(\mathcal{}y(t) = y_{i} + v_{y}(t-t_{i})\)


where \( x_{i}=x(t_i)\) and \( y_{i}=y(t_i)\) are the x and y components of the position vector at the time \( t=t_i\).

The total velocity would then be found by combining the x and y components of the velocity using the standard rules for vector components.

A more interesting case arises when the motion along one coordinate has constant velocity while there is accelerated motion in a perpendicular direction; projectile motion is an example.
