Velocity and Acceleration

The motion of an object with a changing velocity is most elegantly expressed using the language of calculus. In fact, this might be called the original calculus problem, since it motivated Newton to invent calculus.

From instantaneous velocity to instantaneous acceleration

In mathematical terms, velocity is the time derivative of position, and acceleration is the time derivative of velocity:

\( \vec{v}(t) = \frac{d\vec{r}(t)}{dt}\)

\( \vec{a}(t) = \frac{d\vec{v}(t)}{dt}\)

where \(\vec{r}(t)\) is the three dimensional position vector, \(\vec{v}\) is the velocity vector, and \(\vec{a}\) is the acceleration vector.

An equivalent representation is through the integral forms of these equations:

\( \vec{r}(t)=\vec{r}_{i} + \int_{t_{i}}^{t} \vec{v}(\eta)\; d\eta\)

\( \vec{v}(t) = \vec{v}_{i} + \int_{t_{i}}^{t} \vec{a}(\eta)\; d\eta \)

where η is a dummy variable for the integral over time.

It should be noted in these expressions that the integrals are definite integrals that start at the initial time \(t_i\) where the arbitrary constant of integration is replaced by the value of the physical quantity at that time, e.g. \(\vec{r}(t_i)\) or \(\vec{v}(t_i)\).

Recovering the Special Case of Constant Velocity [show]

Suppose \(\vec{a} = 0\). Then, the integral representation becomes:

\( \vec{v}(t) = \vec{v}_{i}\)

(which implies constant velocity) and:

\( \vec{r}(t) = \vec{r}_{i} + \vec{v} \int_{t_{i}}^{t} d\eta = \vec{r}_{i} + \vec{v}(t-t_{i})\)

where we have used the fact that the velocity is constant in evaluating the integral.

If we define our coordinates so that the velocity vector is aligned with the x direction, the final equation is identical to the mathematical representation of the "Constant Velocity (Zero Acceleration) Model".
