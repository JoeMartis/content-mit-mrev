Torque and Rotation About a Fixed Axis

In this unit we begin our discussion of rotational dynamics and rotational motion. We will start by introducing the concept of a rigid body, the new system constituent, and the concept of torque, the new manifestation of an interaction to produce a change in rotational motion.

Rigid Body

Up until now in this course, we have been treating objects as point particles. Point particles have no extent, and thus the only motion they are capable of is translation. Real objects, however, do have extent. They can do more than just translate. In this module we will introduce the next-simplest possible type of system constituent: the rigid body.

A rigid body, unlike a point particle, does have a size and shape. It is a simplification of real solid objects: it has the property that the distance between any two points on the body remains constant – we will not be analyzing deformations like bending, twisting, or stretching. Rigid bodies can still translate like point particles, but they also have one other important type of motion available to them: rotational motion. In other words, rigid bodies can spin.

Torque and Rotational Motion

The concept of torque produced by a force represents a new way of describing the effect of interactions on the motion of an object. This concept will be defined in the next module.

We will be extending the idea from translational motion, namely

Interactions --> Force --> Change in translational motion

to include rotational motion. The net torque applied to a rigid body will produce a change in rotational motion:

Interactions --> Force --> Torque --> Change in rotational motion
