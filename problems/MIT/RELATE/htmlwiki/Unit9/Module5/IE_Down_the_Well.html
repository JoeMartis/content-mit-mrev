Illustrative Example: Down the Well

A bucket for collecting water from a well is suspended by a rope which is wound around a pulley. The empty bucket has a mass of 2.0 kg, and the pulley can be treated as a uniform cylinder of mass 3.0 kg on a frictionless axle. Suppose a person releases the bucket (from rest) into the well.

PART A: What is the bucket's acceleration as it falls? [show]
System: [show]
We will consider two systems related by a constraint (the magnitude of the tension force is the same in both systems):
  1. The bucket is treated as a point particle.
  2. The pulley is treated as a rigid body.
Interactions: [show]
1. Bucket Resulting Forces Point of application
bucket & Earth mbg center of mass
bucket & rope T: tension center of mass
2. Pulley Resulting Forces Point of application
pulley & Earth mpg center of mass
pulley & rope T: tension at a distance R from the center of mass
pulley & axle FAxle: contact force center of mass
Because the mass of the pulley is uniformly distributed, the axle (axis of rotation) passes through the center of mass.
Solution [show]
Diagramatic Representation [show]
We begin with free body diagrams of the two two objects.

Mathematical Representation [show]
For the bucket: we will apply Newton' Second Law to the bucket and write the vectors using a coordinate system with the vertical axis positive downwards, in the same direction of the acceleration:

\(\displaystyle m_{b}g - T = m_{b}a_{b} \)

For the pulley, there will be no translation, only rotation about the center of mass. We sum the torques about the fixed axis defined by the axle. Only the force of tension will produce a torque about the axis, therefore :

\( \displaystyle \sum\tau_{axis}= TR = I_{p}\alpha_{p}\)

where Ip is the moment of inertia of the pulley about the axle, and αp the pulley's angular acceleration. Note that we have followed the right hand rule to indicate the torque direction.

We now make the assumption that the rope does not stretch or slip as it unwinds. This constraint relates the rotation of the pulley to the motion of the bucket:

\( \displaystyle \alpha_{p} R = a_{b} \)

We combine the equations above to obtain:

\(\displaystyle a_{b} = \frac{g}{1+\frac{\displaystyle I_{p}}{\displaystyle m_{b}R^{2}}} \)

Note that \( \frac{\displaystyle I_{p}}{\displaystyle m_{b}R^{2}} \) is *not* equal to 1/2. The ratio of the pulley's moment of intertia to the pulley's mass times radius squared is 1/2, but we have the ratio of the pulley's moment of inertia to the bucket's mass times the pulley's radius squared.

or, using the formula for the moment of inertia of a cylinder:

\( a_{b} = \frac{g}{1+\frac{\displaystyle m_{p}}{\displaystyle 2m_{b}}} = \mbox{5.6 m/s}^{2}\)

Check the answer using the following limiting cases: 1) the acceleration approaches g if the mass of the bucket is infinite (or much much larger than the mass of the pulley), and 2) the acceleration approaches zero if the mass of the pulley goes to infinity (or is much much larger than the mass of the bucket).


Part B: What is the bucket's speed after falling 5.0 m down the well? [show]
System [show]
The bucket treated as a point particle.
Interactions: [show]
1. Bucket Resulting Forces
bucket & Earth mbg
bucket & rope T: tension
Model: [show]
Because the total force on the bucket is constant, the motion is described by:One-Dimensional Motion with Constant Acceleration
Solution: [show]
Using the bucket's acceleration found in part A, we can use the expression for the function of height:

\( v_{y}^{2} = v_{y,{\rm i}}^{2} + 2 a_{y} (y-y_{\rm i})\)

If we assume yi = 0 m at the height of release, then:

\( v_{y} = \pm \sqrt{2 a_{y} y} \)

The speed of the bucket, then, must be:

\( v_{b} = \sqrt{2 (\mbox{5.6 m/s}^{2}) (\mbox{5.0 m})} = \mbox{7.5 m/s} \)
