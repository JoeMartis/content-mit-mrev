Rotational Form of Newton's Second Law and the Moment of Inertia

Learning goals

After working through this module, you should be able to:

  • Apply rotational form of Newton's Second Law in simple problems.
  • Find the moment of inertia of a point mass
  • Compare the moment of inertia of two objects

Newton's Second Law for Pure Rotations


By analogy to \(\sum\vec{F}^{external}=m\vec{a} \) , we can write a similar relationship for rotations around a fixed axis. If we apply a non zero torque on an object (e.g. push or pull on a door handle), it will result in a change of rotational motion (the door will start to rotate).

It can be shown that for an object rotating about an axis that passes through a point Q, the net torque on the object measured about point Q is given by:

\[\sum\vec{\tau}_Q^{external} = I_Q \vec{\alpha} \]

Where \(\alpha\) is the angular acceleration: the rate of change of the angular speed \(\omega\), and \(\displaystyle I_Q\) is the moment of inertia about point Q, a quantity that is analogous to the inertial mass in \( \sum\vec{F}^{external}=m\vec{a} \).

Moment of Inertia


The Moment of inertia describes the resistance of an object to being rotated and depends on how the mass is distributed with respect to the axis of rotation.

Moment of Inertia of a Point Particle


Consider a point particle of mass M moving in a circle of radius R, the moment of inertia of the point particle measured with respect to the center of the circle is given by \( I_Q= M R^2 \), where Q is the point at the center of the circle.


The moment of inertia of a system of more than one point particle can be obtained by adding the moment of inertia of the individual particles. The moment of inertia of N point particles rotating about an axis passing through point Q is given by:


\(I_Q=\sum_{i=1}^{i=N}m_ir_i^2\)


where mi is the mass of particle i and ri is the distance between the particle i and the axis passing through point Q.

Finding the moment of inertia of an extended object requires an integral.

To find the moment of inertia of a rigid object, we essentially consider the object as a collection of tiny lumps. Then we figure out how far the lumps are from the axis of rotation and perform a sum over the lumps.

\( I= \text{sum of: lumps of mass} \times (\text{the distance from the axis of rotation})^2 \)


In the infinitesimal limit, the sum process over all the lumps becomes an integration, and the expression for the moment of inertia becomes:

\( I_Q =\int r^{2}\; dm \)

Most of the time you do not need to perform the actual integration. However if you do need to do that, you should notice that:

  • dm represents the small lump of mass. In practicality, the mass of the small lump is its volume multiplied by the density, so it is usually expressed in term of ρdxdydz or ρr2drdθdz or ρr2sin2(θ)drdθdφ. The choice of which one to use depends on the symmetry of the object.
  • After you do all the above, you need to express r , the distance of that lump to the axis, in terms of the coordinate system that you pick.
  • This is a definite integral: you are summing over the object, and the limits are usually the boundaries of the object.
