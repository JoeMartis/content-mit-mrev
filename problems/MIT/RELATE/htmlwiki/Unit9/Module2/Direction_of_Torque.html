Finding the Direction of the Torque

The torque is a vector whose direction (from the definition of vector product) is perpendicular to the position and force vectors. Equivalently, the torque is perpendicular to the plane defined by these two vectors.

In this course we will by convention place our rigid bodies at z=0 (in the x-y plane) and apply forces that act only in this plane. Since both the position vector \(\vec{r}\) and the force \(\vec{F}\) lie in the x-y plane, the torque will always point in the z-direction, either along the +z axis or -z axis. The determination is made using the right hand rule for torque:

 

The Right Hand Rule for Torque
1. Place the fingers of your right hand along the direction of vector \(\vec{r}\),
2. Curl them towards vector \(\vec{F}\),
3. Your thumb is now pointing in the direction of the torque.

Conventions for the direction of torque

Because of our restriction to rotation in a plane, we can describe torque as being "positive" or "negative" as long as we are consistent with the (x,y,z) right handed coordinate system.

Why not just say that "counterclockwise" is positive?

Whether an object has torque in the clockwise or counterclockwise direction depends on where you are standing. The direction of the torque vector does not. It may help to imagine looking at an analog clock from the back. The hands will be "moving backwards" from your point of view, with each one rotating counterclockwise, but the vector direction of its motion is still the same. Try it with the right-hand rule!
