Illustrative Example: The ladder problem

A ladder standing against a wall; what conditions will suffice to prevent it from sliding?

Part A: A 3.0 m long ladder is leaned against a vertical wall at a 70° angle above the horizontal. The wall is frictionless, but the (level) floor beneath the ladder does have friction. Assuming the center of mass of the 15.0 kg ladder is in its exact center, find the forces exerted on the ladder by the wall and by the floor. [show]
System: [show]
The ladder as a rigid body.
Interactions: [show]
External influences from the Earth (gravity) , the wall (normal force) and the floor (normal force and frictional force).
Diagramatic Representation [show]

This is a statics problem, so the object is to ensure that the ladder does not rotate or translate. Thus, we know that all accelerations and angular accelerations are zero. We first sketch the situation and set up coordinates, which includes selecting an axis. Selecting the position of the axis is technically arbitrary, but often it is actually specified by the unknowns in the system. In this case, as we shall see, the forces in the x-direction are less constrained than the y-direction forces. Thus, it is a good idea to position the axis to cancel the torque from one of the x forces. We choose to put the axis at the point of contact with the floor.
Mathematical Representation [show]
We then write the equations of linear and rotational equilibrium:

\( \sum F_{x} = F_{w} + F_{f,x} = 0 \)

\( \sum F_{y} = - mg + F_{f,y} = 0 \)

\( \sum \tau = + mg (L/2) \cos\theta - F_{w} L \sin\theta \)

Note that our selection of the axis point guaranteed that force due to the floor's contribute no torque.

The 1st and 3rd lines (y-component and torque equations) contain only one unknown quantity. We solve for the unknown:

\( \displaystyle F_{f,y} = mg = \mbox{147 N}\)

\( \displaystyle F_{w} = \frac{mg}{2}\cot\theta = \mbox{27 N}\)

Once we have \(F_{w}\), we can substitute into the x-component equation to find:

\( \displaystyle F_{f,x} = -F_{w} = - \mbox{27 N}\)

We can now construct the total floor contact force, as desired:


Which gives a total force of 150 N at 80° above the horizontal.
Part B: Consider the situation if we add friction between the wall and the ladder. If the coefficient of static friction between the floor and the ladder is 0.15, what is the minimum coefficient of static friction needed between the wall and the ladder to prevent the ladder from slipping? [show]
System: [show]
The ladder as a rigid body.
Interactions: [show]
External influences from the Earth (gravity) , the wall (normal force and friction) and the floor (normal force and friction).
Critical insight! [show]
The minimum coefficient of friction will occur when both friction forces are maximal (as large as they can be). Then:

\( \displaystyle |F_{f,x}| = \mu_{s,f} |F_{f,y}|\)

\( \displaystyle |F_{w,y}| = \mu_{s,w} |F_{w,x}| \)
Diagramatic Representation [show]

Because we know the floor's static friction coefficient, we can simplify the solution by a revised choice of axis. Putting the axis at the point of contact with the wall will eliminate any torque due to the unknown friction force there.
Mathematical Representation [show]
The resulting equations are:

\( \sum F_{x} = F_{w,x} - \mu_{s,f}F_{f,y} = 0 \)

\( \sum F_{y} = - mg + F_{f,y} + \mu_{s,w}F_{w,x}= 0 \)

\( \sum \tau = - mg (L/2) \cos\theta + F_{f,y} L\cos\theta - \mu_{s,f}F_{f,y} L \sin\theta \)

Note that the y-component of the floor force is the normal force from the floor on the ladder, and the x-component of the wall force is the normal force from the wall on the ladder. Since we are assuming static friction is maximized, we have rewritten \( F_{f,x} \) and \( F_{w,y} \) as the coefficient of friction times the relevant normal force.

The only equation that can be solved immediately is torque balance, which gives:

\(F_{f,y} = \frac{mg}{2\left(1-\mu_{s,f}\tan\theta\right)} = \mbox{125 N} \)

We can then substitute this result into the equation of x-force balance to find:

\( \displaystyle F_{w,x} = \mu_{s,f} \cdot F_{f,y} = \mbox{18.8 N} \)

Finally, we can find the required coefficient of static friction to be:

\( \mu_{s,w} = \frac{mg-F_{f,y}}{F_{w,x}} = 1.19 \)

Sense-making: From the friction due to the floor computed in Part A, we can deduce that, even with a frictionless wall, the ladder is stable as long as the coefficient of friction with the ground is 0.19 or greater (check this). Now we have set the floor coefficient to 0.15 and found that the wall friction coefficient must be at least 1.19. Does it make sense that in the wall's friction coefficient has to be so much larger than the minimum floor coefficient (~0.19) if the wall is only partly responsible for keeping the ladder from sliding?
