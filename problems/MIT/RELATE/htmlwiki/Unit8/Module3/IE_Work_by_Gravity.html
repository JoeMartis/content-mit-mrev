Illustrative Example: Work Done by the Gravitational Force.

We will calculate the work done by the gravitational force in the two situations:

  • Gravity near the Earth's surface: The gravitational force is constant, though the path may change direction.
  • Gravity far from the Earth's surface: The gravitational force depends on position.

Note: In this example we use the gravitational force as another force to illustrate the procedure for calculating work. Because the gravitational force is conservative it is more common to use the change of gravitational potential energy instead of the gravitational work.


Example 1. The work done by near-Earth gravity.

The control of a model airplane of mass m is not working properly. The airplane starts to descend from an initial height yi, to a final height yf as shown in the figure. What is the work done by the gravitational force as the airplane goes from point \(\vec{r}_i\) to point \(\vec{r}_f\)?

Answer [show]

Consider the coordinate system shown in the figure. The force due to gravity, \(\vec{F}_g=-mg\hat{j}\), is constant. The displacement vector, \(d\vec{r} = dx\hat{i} + dy\hat{j}\), is changing direction along the path.

The dot product between the gravitational force and the displacement vector is:

\(\vec{F}d\vec{r} = -mg\hat{j}(dx\hat{i} + dy\hat{j}) = -mgdy\)

Then, the work is given by:

\(W_{i,f}=\int_{\vec{r}_i}^{\vec{r}_f}(-mgdy) = -mgy|_{y_i}^{y_{f}}=-mg(y_f - y_i)\)

Note:

  • Because the plane is descending then yf < yi and Wi,f > 0 as expected. In this case, work done by the gravitational force will increase the kinetic energy of the plane.
  • The result is still valid if the airplane is moving up. In this case, yf > yi and the Wi,f < 0. The negative work means the kinetic energy will decrease.



Example 2. Work done by far-Earth gravity

The space shuttle is changing orbit by following the path shown in the figure. What is the work done by the gravitational force on the shuttle between points \(\vec{r}_i\) and \(\vec{r}_f\)?

Answer [show]

The magnitude of the gravitational force is given by:

\(F_g = G\frac{m_Em}{r}\)

where r is the distance between the center of mass of the Earth and the shuttle. The gravitational force points towards the center of mass of the Earth (considered fixed because the mass of the Earth mE >> m).

As shown in the figure, the displacement vector can be decomposed into two components, one along the radial direction and one tangential to the circle of radius r. Note that the gravitational force is aligned with the radial component only. Therefore, after taking the dot product, only the radial component of the displacement vector contributes to the work. Using polar coordinates, \(d\vec{r} = dr\hat{r} + rd\theta\hat{\theta}\).

The dot product:

\(\vec{F}_g \cdot d\vec{r} = -G\frac{m_Em}{r}dr\)

The work done by gravity:

\( W_{i,f}=\int_{\vec{r}_i}^{\vec{r}_f}\vec{F}_g \cdot \;d\vec{r} = -Gm_Em\int_{\vec{r}_i}^{\vec{r}_f}\frac{dr}{r} = G\frac{m_Em}{r}|_{\vec{r}_i}^{\vec{r}_f}\)

\( W_{i,f}= G\frac{m_Em}{r_f} - G\frac{m_Em}{r_i}\)

Note:

  • When the shuttle is moving to a larger orbit, rf > ri, and W_i,f < 0 , which makes sense because the gravitational force points opposite to the radial component of displacement vector. The work causes the kinetic energy of the shuttle to decrease.
  • The result above is also valid if the shuttle moves to a smaller orbit. In this case, rf < ri and Wi,f > 0 . Because the gravitational force points in the same direction as the radial component of the displacement, the work causes the kinetic energy to increase.
