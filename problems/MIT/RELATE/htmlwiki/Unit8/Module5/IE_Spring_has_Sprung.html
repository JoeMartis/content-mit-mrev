Illustrative Example: Spring has Sprung

A certain spring-loaded gun is cocked by compressing its spring by 5.0 cm. The gun fires a 4.0 g projectile with a speed of 8.0 m/s. What spring constant is required for the spring?

Solution [show]
System: [show system]
The projectile, treated as a point particle, plus the gun treated as an infinitely massive object.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The conservative spring interaction between the gun and the projectile will give rise to elastic potential energy.

Model: [show Model]
Constant Mechanical Energy.

Approach: [show approach]

Assumptions:
  1. We will ignore friction and other non-conservative interactions, which means that the mechanical energy of the system will be constant.
  2. We will further make the usual assumption that the projectile stops interacting with the spring when the spring returns to its equilibrium position (the projectile has essentially been "fired" at that point)
  3. By treating the gun as an infinitely massive object we are considering that after the firing the gun does not move, therefore all the final kinetic energy corresponds to the projectile.


Step 1: Draw an Initial State-Final State Diagram [show]

The initial state corresponds to the instant when the spring is compressed a distance xi and the projectile is at rest:

Ki = 0

\( U_i = 1/2kx_{i}^{2}\)

The final state corresponds to the instant when the spring is back to its equilibrium position and the projectile is moving at vf:

\(K_f = 1/2mv_{f}^{2}\)

Uf = 0


Step 2: Represent the Model Mathematically [show]
We can now express the fact that the mechanical energy is constant through the Law of Change:

\( K_{f} + U_{f} = K_{i} + U_{i}\;\)

After removing zeros, we have:

\( K_{f} = U_{i}\;\)

Giving:

\( 1/2mv_{f}^2 = 1/2kx_{i}^2 \)

Solving for k:

\( k =\frac{mv_{f}^2}{x_{i}^2} = (\mbox{102 N/m});\)
