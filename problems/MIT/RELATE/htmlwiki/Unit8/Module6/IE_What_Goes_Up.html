Illustrative Example: What Goes Up

A person heaves a 5.0 kg object straight up into the air. The object is released at a height of 1.0 m above the ground, and reaches a maximum height of 1.0 m above the release point. The object then falls all the way to the ground. What is the kinetic energy of the object at the instant of release and at the instant it hits the ground?

Solution [show]
System: [show]
The 5.0 kg object, treated as a point particle, plus the earth, treated as an infinitely massive object.
Interactions: [show]
The object plus the earth generate an internal gravitational interaction.
Model: [show]
Constant sum of kinetic plus gravitational potential energy.
Quantitative Approach: [show]
We will solve this problem using two different possible definitions of the gravitational potential energy for the system.
Method 1: Set y = 0 at ground level. [show]
  • Step 1: Plot the gravitational potential energy curve for this situation.
  • Step 2: Determine where the object has zero speed. This will occur when the object has reached maximum height. Draw a horizontal line that touches the potential energy curve at this point. This curve will represent the total energy, since zero speed means zero kinetic energy. In this problem, zero speed will occur at y = 2.0 m, since that is the maximum height reached by the object above the ground, assuming that y = 0 at ground level.

  • Step 3: Fill in energy bars for all points of interest. In this case, the principle points of interest are at a height of 0.0 m (the point at which the object hits the ground) and a height of 1.0 m (the height at which the object was released). The energy bars must always add up to the total energy. The height of the potential energy bar is simply the value of the potential energy curve at the appropriate location.

  • Step 4: We can now simply read off the appropriate kinetic energies. At the moment of impact with the ground, the object has 98 J of kinetic energy. At the instant of release, it has 49 J.


Method 2: Set y = 0 at release height. [show]

Another completely equivalent description of the motion can be obtained by choosing coordinates such that y = 0 at the height of release.

  • Step 1: Plot the gravitational potential energy curve for this situation.
  • Step 2: Determine where the object has zero speed. This will occur when the object has reached maximum height. Draw a horizontal line that touches the potential energy curve at this point. This curve will represent the total energy, since zero speed means zero kinetic energy. In this problem, zero speed will occur at y = 1.0 m, since that is the maximum height reached by the object above the ground, assuming that y = 0 at ground level.

Note that this gives less total energy than the first method (y = 0 at ground level).

  • Step 3: Fill in energy bars for all points of interest. In this case, the principle points of interest are at a height of -1.0 m (the point at which the object hits the ground) and a height of 0.0 m (the height at which the object was released). The energy bars must always add up to the total energy. The height of the potential energy bar is simply the value of the potential energy curve at the appropriate location.

Note that at y = -1.0 m, the potential energy is negative.

  • Step 4: We can now simply read off the appropriate kinetic energies. At the moment of impact with the ground, the object has 98 J of kinetic energy. At the instant of release, it has 49 J. These kinetic energies are exactly the same as those obtained with y = 0 at ground level. Thus, even though we have redefined the potential energy and the total energy of the system, the description of the motion (the kinetic energy) is unchanged!
