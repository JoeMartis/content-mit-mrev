Gravitational Potential Energy

Potential Energy of the Near Earth Gravity
\( U^{g}(y) = mgy\;\)

where y is the height of the object of mass m.

Potential Energy of the far Earth Gravity
\(U^g(r) = -G\frac{m_Em}{r} \)

wherer is the distance between the center of mass of the Earth and the object, mE is the mass of the Earth, and G = 6.67 x 10-11 N.m2/kg2 is the gravitational constant.

Near Earth Gravity [show]
The work done by the near Earth gravitational force on an object of mass m was calculated in the previous module. Assuming a positive axis pointing up and gravity pointing down, then the work is given by: Wi,f = − mgh(yf − yi) where yf, yi are the final and initial height of the object. The change in potential energy of the object is obtained as:

U(yf) = U(yi) − Wi,f

or

\( U(y_f) = U(y_i) + mg(y_{f} - y_{i})\;\)

Setting up a reference for the potential energy

Above we have defined the change of potential energy. We can also define the potential energy of an object if we set a reference value for the potential energy. In this case, we can set the potential energy to be zero at the surface, U(y = 0) = 0, then the potential energy becomes:

U(y) = mgy

Universal Gravity [show]
The work done by the gravitational force on an object of mass m far from the Earth's surface was obtained in the previous module:

\( W_{i,f} = Gm_Em(\frac{1}{r_f}-\frac{1}{r_i})\)

where rf,ri are the final and initial distance between the object and the center of the Earth. The change in potential energy of the object is:

U(rf) = U(ri) − Wi,f

or

\( U(r_f) = U(r_i) - Gm_Em(\frac{1}{r_f} - \frac{1}{r_i})\)


Note:

When an object is moving away from the Earth, rf > ri and Wi,f < 0, so the kinetic energy decreases. By the same account, the potential energy increases. The result of the work is a change of kinetic energy into potential energy.

Setting up a reference for the potential energy

Above we have defined the change of potential energy. If we set a the initial position to be a reference point (like the origin of a coordinate system), then we can define the potential energy of an object relative to this point. In the case of far-Earth gravitation, we choose to set the potential energy to be zero at infinity: \(U(r_i)= 0\). If the final position is given by the radial distance \(r\), then the expression above reduces to:

\( U(r) = -G\frac{m_E m}{r}\)
