Describing Motion that Combines Rotation and Translation

It is not required that an object have a fixed axle or hinges in order to spin. You can spin essentially any object. Almost any sport will provide an excellent example of a spinning and translating object. In some sports, that object is a ball or implement. In others, the "object" is a person!

Learning Goals

After completing this unit you should be able to:

  • Explain why the translation and rotation of a system can be described as the translation of the center of mass and the rotation about an axis passing through the center of mass.
  • Determine the translational and rotational motion of a system in terms of the external forces and torques applied.
  • Apply the constraint of rolling without slipping

Translation Of and Rotation About Center of Mass

For a rigid body that is free to rotate and translate, the motion of the object can be thought of as a combination of translation of the center of mass and rotation about the center of mass. For a truly rigid body that cannot deform at all, the combination of translation of the center of mass plus spin about the center of mass are sufficient to reproduce any possible real motion.

Systems that are free to translate and rotate can simultaneously experience translational acceleration of their center of mass and angular acceleration about an axis through the center of mass. In this case, both Newton's Second Law and the equivalent expression for rotational dynamics will each be satisfied. In other words, the equations:


\(\sum{\vec{F}^{ext}}=M\vec{a}_{cm}\)

\(\sum{\tau}^{ext}_{cm} = I_{cm} \alpha\)

can be applied simultaneously.
