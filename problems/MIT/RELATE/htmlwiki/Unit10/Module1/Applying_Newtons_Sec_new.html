Dynamics of Rigid Bodies: Rotation and Translation

Learning Goals

After finishing this unit you should be able to:

  • Solve problems involving a rigid body that is both translating and rotating about an axis that passes through its center of mass, when forces act on that body.
  • Apply constraints to connect rotation to translation.

This unit does not cover rigid bodies translating in three dimensions, or rotating about axes that are not fixed.

Approach

In general we want to apply Newtons Second Law to the motion of the center of mass (as a point), and also apply the equivalent of Newton's Second Law for rotation.

\( \displaystyle \sum F=m a_{CM} \)

and

\( \displaystyle \sum \tau_{CM}=I_{CM} \alpha \)

These relationships are very general and powerful. It doesn't matter where on the body the forces act, and the angular motion is independent of whether the center of mass is accelerating.

Independence of Rotation and Translation

To demonstrate this independence, let's look at an example.

The image below shows a rod with a force applied. You know that the rod will accelerate. Because the force is applied directly toward the object's center, it will not rotate.

If we move the force off-center, the rod will rotate. However, it will also accelerate the exact same amount that it did before. Why?

Force is defined as an object's mass times its acceleration. It doesn't matter where we apply that force, the acceleration will be the same. The force is not "used up" to create torque. We get the torque "for free" with our force.

It may not be easy to keep applying force in the same way on a rotating object, but if we can, we will get the same acceleration as we would on a non-rotating object.
