Illustrative Example: A Disk Rolling Down a Ramp

A disk is rolling without slipping down an incline. What is the relationship between the acceleration of the center of mass and the angular acceleration of the disk?
Answer [show]
Drawing the free body diagram of the disk:

In this situation, the motion of the disk can be decomposed in two parts:

Motion of the center of mass: \( \displaystyle \vec{F}^{net}=m \vec{a}_{CM} \) [show]
We consider the disk as a point mass concentrated at the center of mass and moving down the incline, the acceleration of the center of mass is determined by Newtons Second Law. The forces acting on the disk are gravity and static friction. Notice that the magnitude of static friction is not known, it has to be great enough to prevent the disk from slipping. Setting up a coordinate system as in the picture and looking at the net force down the incline:

\( \displaystyle mg \sin ( \theta) -f_{static} =m a_{CM} \)

Motion around the center of mass: \( \displaystyle \tau^{net}=I_{CM} \alpha \) [show]
Now we look at the rotation around the center of mass. The angular acceleration is determined by the net torque. We can choose the axis of rotation to go through the center of mass. The net torque is the sum of the torque due to gravity, the torque due to the normal and the torque due to the static friction. The gravitational and normal force do not produce any torque about the disks center of mass (zero moment arm). Only static friction is able to produce a torque. Following the right hand rule, the torque due to friction is into the screen, then the net torque has magnitude \( \displaystyle f_{static} R \).

We choose a coordinate system where rotation in the clockwise direction is positive, and we have:

\( \displaystyle f_{static} R =I_{CM} \alpha \)

In order to use the above relationships, we need to connect the two motions. This is usually a result of a constraint, which is in this case the fact that the disk does not slip.

Relationship between \(\displaystyle a_{CM} \) and \(\displaystyle \alpha \) [show]
To figure out the relationship between \(\displaystyle a_{CM} \) and \( \displaystyle\alpha \), we look at the motion of a point on the rim of the disk relative to the center of mass. If we take the point of contact with the surface, this point is stationary (always fixed). In the same frame, the center of mass moves with an acceleration \(\displaystyle a_{CM} \).

Now if we shift our view to the frame where the center of mass is stationary, the point of contact with the surface will appear moving backwards with an acceleration equal in magnitude \(\displaystyle a_{CM} \). Now we can use the fact that the tangential acceleration of a point on a rigid body is related to the angular acceleration by \( \displaystyle a_{tan}= R \alpha \), where R is the distance between the axis of rotation and that point. putting all together:

\( \displaystyle a_{CM}= R \alpha \)
