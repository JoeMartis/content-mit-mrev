Illustrative Example: Down The Ramp

Consider a ball placed on a ramp inclined at θ = 30° above the horizontal.

Part A: What is the acceleration of the ball's center of mass as it rolls down the ramp? Assume that the ball rolls without slipping. [show]
System: [show]
The ball is treated as a rigid body.
Interactions: [show]
External influences from the earth (gravity) and the ramp (friction and normal force).
Diagramatic Representation [show]

The ball will translate and rotate as it rolls down the slope. The relevant free body diagram is shown below.

Mathematical Representation [show]
From this free body diagram we can construct Newton's 2nd Law for the ball's center of mass and also the angular version of Newton's 2nd Law for rotations of the ball about the center of mass. The x-component of Newton's 2nd Law is:

\( \displaystyle \sum F_{x} = mg \sin\theta - f_{static} = ma_{x} \)

and the sum of torques about the center of mass is:

\( \displaystyle \sum {\tau}_{cm} = Rf_{static} = I\alpha \)

It is not appropriate to assume that the friction force is equal to μN. The ball is moving, but the point of contact with the ground does not slide relative to the surface, and hence we are in the static friction regime.

We can eliminate the friction force from the two equations to find:

\( \displaystyle mg \sin \theta = ma_{x} + I\frac{\alpha}{R}\)

Now, because the ball is rolling without slipping, we can relate the angular acceleration to the linear acceleration of the center of mass: \( \displaystyle \alpha R = a_{x} \)

Substituting this expression allows us to express the acceleration as:

\( \displaystyle a = \frac{g \sin\theta}{1 + \frac{\displaystyle I}{\displaystyle mR^{2}}} \)

Using the result that the moment of inertia for a sphere about an axis that passes through its center of mass is 2/5 m R2, we have:

\( \displaystyle a =\frac{g \sin\theta}{1+\frac{\displaystyle 2}{\displaystyle 5}} = \frac{5 g \sin(30^{\circ})}{7} = \mbox{3.5 m/s} \)

Note that we have been given no information whatever about the mass or radius of the ball! The acceleration is independent of the mass and the radius.

Part B:Assuming the ball is released from rest, what is the speed of the ball's center of mass after it has moved 1.3 m along the ramp? [show]
System: [show]
The ball is treated as a rigid body.
Interactions: [show]
External influences from the earth (gravity) and the ramp (friction and normal force).
Approach [show]
After using one of the methods described in Part A to determine the acceleration, we can use kinematics to find the speed. The most direct approach is to use:


\( \displaystyle v_{x}^{2} = v_{x,{\rm i}}^{2} + 2 a_{x} (x-x_{\rm i}) \)

where we choose \(x_i=0\) m. Solving gives:


\( \displaystyle v_{x} = \pm \sqrt{2a_{x}x } = \pm \sqrt{\frac{2gx\sin\theta}{1+\frac{\displaystyle I}{\displaystyle mR^{2}}}} \)

we choose the plus sign, since the ball is translating in the +x direction. Since the y-velocity is zero, the total speed of the ball's center of mass is:


\( \displaystyle v = \mbox{3.0 m/s} \)
