Illustrative Examples: Mass on a Spring

Suppose you have a 0.500 kg mass attached to a horizontal spring with a spring constant of 30.5 N/m. The other end of the spring is secured to a wall. The 0.500 kg mass is free to slide on a level, frictionless surface. The following problems relate to this situation.

Part A: Suppose the mass is pushed so that the spring is compressed by 2.50 cm from its equilibrium length. The mass is held at rest for an instant, and then released. How fast is the mass moving when the spring has returned to its equilibrium length? [show]
We will illustrate the utility of mechanical energy conservation by solving this problem in two ways:
Method 1: Use the Simple Harmonic Motion Model [show]
System: [show system]
The oscillating mass is approximated as a point particle.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The gravitational interaction with the earth and the normal force produced by the ground cancel each other, leaving only the elastic restoring interaction of the spring as a relevant interaction.

Models: [show Models]
Simple Harmonic Motion Model.

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

Define the Initial Conditions [show]
The integrated equations of motion for the Simple Harmonic Motion Model are:

\[x = A\cos(\omega_{\rm osc} t) + B \sin(\omega_{\rm osc} t)\]

and

\[v_{x} = -A\omega_{\rm osc} \sin(\omega_{\rm osc} t) B\omega_{\rm osc}\cos(\omega_{\rm osc} t)\]

Based upon the information given in the problem statement, we know that x(0 s) = 2.50 cm if we take positive x values to denote compression and assume that the release of the mass occurs at t = 0. We are also informed that the mass is released at rest, so v(0) = 0. Thus:

\[x(0) = 2.50\;{\rm cm} = A\cos(0) + B\sin(0) = A\]

and

\[v_{x}(0) = 0 = \omega_{\rm osc}(-A\sin(0)+B\cos(0)) = \omega_{\rm osc} B\]

Thus, B = 0 and A = 2.5 cm meaning that form of the equations of motion is reduced to:

\[x = A\cos(\omega_{\rm osc} t)\]

and

\[v_{x} = -\omega_{\rm osc}A\sin(\omega_{\rm osc} t)\]

Substitute in the Appropriate Time [show]
We are asked to find the speed of the mass at equilibrium, which is necessarily at the point x = 0 based upon the form of the equations we have used. To evaluate the speed, we need to know the time at which the mass reaches x = 0. We can find the time by using our equation for position. We must solve:

\(x = 0 = A\cos(\omega_{\rm osc} t)\;\)

This equation has an infinite number of solutions:

\( t = \left\{ \frac{\pi}{2\omega_{\rm osc}}, \frac{3\pi}{2\omega_{\rm osc}}, \frac{5\pi}{2\omega_{\rm osc}},\ldots \right\}\)

This is a consequence of the conservation of energy in the motion. If we ignore dissipative forces, the oscillation will persist forever, and so equilibrium will be attained an infinite number of times. The natural question, then, is which of these to pick? Of course, the conservation of energy also suggests the resolution to this difficulty: if energy is perfectly conserved, it will not matter which we pick. The speed will always be the same at equilibrium.

The Form of the Speed [show]
Since all the possible equilibrium times should give the same speed, we choose the first for simplicity. This gives:

\[v_{x}\left(t=\pi/(2\omega_{\rm osc})\right) = -\omega_{\rm osc} A \sin\left(\pi/(2\omega_{\rm osc})\right) = -\omega_{\rm osc} A\]

which is negative. This negative sign is irrelevant for the speed (which is a magnitude) but it does have a meaning. Although the speed at equilibrium will always be the same, the velocity will not be. The mass will first pass through the equilibrium point moving away from the wall, then back toward, then away, then toward, etc. Thus, the sign of the velocity will alternate at each crossing of equilibrium. The size, however, will always be:

\( |v_{x}| = \omega_{\rm osc} A\;\)

Determine the Angular Frequency [show]
Up until now, we have written our equations in terms of the angular frequency of the oscillation. This is not an explicitly given quantity, but we know that for harmonic oscillations:

\[\omega_{\rm osc} = \sqrt{k/m} \]

where k is the spring constant and m is the oscillating mass. Thus, for this problem:

\[\omega_{\rm osc} = \sqrt{\frac{30.5\; {\rm N/m}}{0.500\; {\rm kg}}} = 7.81\;{\rm rad/s}\]

Substitute Numbers [show]
We can now evaluate the speed at equilibrium:

\[ v_{\rm osc} = (7.81 \;{\rm rad/s})\times(0.025 \;{\rm m}) = 0.195\;{\rm m/s}\]

Method 2: Use the Conservation of Energy Model [show]
System: [show system]
The oscillating mass is approximated as a point particle and the wall is approximated as an infinitely massive object.

Interactions: [show interactions]
The gravitational interaction with the earth and the >normal force produced by the ground cancel each other, leaving only the elastic restoring interaction of the spring as a relevant interaction. This elastic interaction is conservative, and so will be represented as a potential energy.

Models: [show Models]
Constant Mechanical Energy (Zero Non-Conservative Work).

Quantitative Approach: [show approach]

Draw an Initial State - Final State Diagram [show]
When using mechanical energy conservation, the most important step is to completely understand the initial and final states of the system. We are interested in the initial state where the mass has just been released from rest and the spring is compressed by 2.50 cm from its equilibrium position. The final state will be when the spring has relaxed back to equilibrium. The picture below shows these two conditions.


Write the Equation of Mechanical Energy Conservation [show]
Using the initial state-final state diagram as a guide, we can construct a mathematical expression for the energy at each of these points and set them equal. The full set of possible energies are kinetic energy of the mass, kinetic energy of the wall, and potential energy in the spring interaction. Thus, the most general possible form of the conservation equation is:

\[K_{{\rm mass},i} + K_{{\rm wall},i} + U_{e,i} = K_{{\rm mass},f} + K_{{\rm wall}, f} + U_{e,f}\]

Of course, our initial state-final state diagram has shown that several of these quantities are zero. Based upon our picture of the situation, we can simplify the equation to:

\[U_{e,i} = K_{{\rm mass},f} \]

Solve for Speed [show]
The speed of the mass can now be found by expanding the form of the kinetic and potential energies:

\[\frac{1}{2}kx_{i}^{2} = \frac{1}{2} m v_{f}^{2}\]

meaning:

\( v_{f} = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}x_{i}^{2}} \)

Substitute Numbers [show]
We know all the quantities in our expression for the speed, so we can substitute to find:

\[v_{f} = \sqrt{\frac{30.5\; {\rm N/m}}{0.500\; {\rm kg}}\times(0.025\;{\rm m})^{2}} = 0.195\;{\rm m/s}\]

Understand the Answer [show]
This is the same answer obtained using the Simple Harmonic Motion Model. The mathematics needed to reach the solution are much simpler using this second method (Conservation of Mechanical Energy).
Part B: Suppose the mass is pushed so that the spring is compressed by 2.50 cm from its equilibrium length. The mass is held at rest for an instant, and then released. How much time elapses from the instant the mass is released until the instant the spring has returned to its equilibrium length? [show]
This time, the Mechanical Energy Model cannot be used for a solution. There is, however, a "short cut" to a solution by using the symmetry of the system.
Warning: You Cannot Find Time with the Constant Mechanical Energy Model [show]
Mechanical energy does not involve time as an explicit variable. Thus, conservation of mechanical energy is not useful in solving this problem. Mechanical energy is most useful in relating the speed of system constituents to the physical configuration of the system.
Method 1: Use the Simple Harmonic Motion Model [show]
The sub-Models of Dynamics and Net Force do explicitly involve time. In fact, this is one reason that the solution to Part A was so tedious using the Simple Harmonic Motion Model. Before finding the speed at a given position (equilibrium) we had to determine the time at which that position was attained. Thus, we had to solve Part B in order to solve Part A! The answer can be found by looking at the solution to Part A. It was:

\[ t_{eq} = \frac{\pi}{2\omega_{\rm osc}}\]

which can be evaluated to obtain:

\[ t_{eq} = \frac{3.14159}{2(7.81\;{\rm rad/s})} = 0.201\;{\rm s}\]

Method 2: Use the symmetry of the motion. [show]
This particular problem can also be solved without going through the full mathematical procedure outlined in the solution to Part A, if the symmetries of the solution to the Simple Harmonic Motion Model are understood. A plot of the position as a function of time for this mass will be a cosine function (as shown in the solution to Part A). Each period of the cosine function can be divided into four equal-time segments as shown below. The first of those segments is the portion of the motion we are interested in, namely the movement from the initial position to equilibrium.

Once we have decided that each of these segments will be 1/4 period in length because of the symmetry of the motion, the time to travel to equilibrium can be found easily. Since the full period is:

\[ T = \frac{2\pi}{\omega_{\rm osc}}\]

we know that 1/4 period will be:

\[T/4 = \frac{\pi}{2\omega_{\rm osc}}\]

which is exactly the answer obtained using the full mathematical solution.

Understand the Answer [show]
Although we said above that conservation of energy is powerless to solve a problem involving time, this solution using symmetry might reasonably be called a conservation of energy solution. The conservation of energy says that the speed of the mass depends only on the potential energy in the system, which is dictated by the position of the mass. It is guaranteed by this relationship that the mass will take just as long to go from equilibrium out to maximum position as it does to return. Further, the symmetry of Hooke's Law for the elastic restoring interaction dictates that there is no difference between maximum + or - position.
