Motion with constant velocity

If the net force acting on an object is zero then there is no change in motion and the object moves with a constant velocity.

If the velocity is constant, then the magnitude as well as the direction of the velocity are constant. As a result, the object moves along a straight line (1-dimensional motion). Selecting the x-axis to be parallel to the object's motion then its velocity is written as:

\(v=\frac{dx}{dt}\)

and the position as a function of time is given by:

\(x(t)=x_i+v(t-t_i)\)

where \(x_i=x(t_i)\) is the position of the object at time \(t_i\). Note that both \(x_i\) and \(t_i\) must be given in order for \(x(t)\) to be well defined.

The motion of an object moving under the action of a zero net force is described by the Constant Velocity Model.

Graphical representation of \(x(t)\) [show]

If \(v\) = constant then \(x(t)=x(t_i)+v(t_i - t)\)

As a result, the position is a linear function of time and the slope of the straight line is the velocity:

\(v=\frac{x(t)-x(t_i)}{t-t_i}\)

Note: Note that the average velocity for any time interval is equal to the instantaneous velocity and is given by the slope of the line.

Derivation of \(x(t)\) [show]

If the velocity is known, the position at a given instant of time \(t\) is obtained by integration of \(v(t)=\frac{dx}{dt}\):

\(x(t)=x(t_i)+\int_{t_i}^{t}v(t')dt'\)

where \(x(t_i)\) is the position of the object at time \(t_i\). To have \(x(t)\) fully determined \(x_i\) and \(t_i\) must be given. They are called the initial conditions.

For the case of constant velocity, \(v\) does not depend on time. As a result the above expression becomes:

\(x(t)=x(t_i)+v\int_{t_i}^{t}dt' = x(t_i)+v(t - t_i)\)
