Technically, there is a gravitational interaction between every pair of massive objects in the universe. The force is proportional to the masses of the bodies, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. This interaction dominates the dynamics of astronomical bodies.

Gravitational Force (Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation)

Between any two point particles of mass m1 and m2, respectively, there will exist an attractive force along the line joining the masses. The force on body 1 due to body 2 will have the form:

$ \vec{F}^{G}_{12} = -G\frac{m_{1}m_{2}}{r_{12}^{2}} \hat{r}_{12}$

where $\vec{r}_{12}$ is the position of object 1 as measured from an origin located at the position of object 2, and G is the gravitational constant which is equal to:

$G = 6.67\times 10^{-11} \mbox{N m}^{2}/\mbox{kg}^{2}$

The Law of Universal Gravitation is compatible with Newton's Third Law of motion, since, by definition:

$ \vec{r}_{12} = \vec{r}_{1} - \vec{r}_{2} = -(\vec{r}_{2} - \vec{r}_{1}) = -\vec{r}_{21}$

(where $\vec{r}_{1}$ and $\vec{r}_{2}$ are the positions of object 1 and 2 in an arbitrary coordinate system) and this implies:

$ \vec{F}^{G}_{12} = - \vec{F}^{G}_{21}$

The Case of Spherical Symmetry

Although the form of Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation is strictly valid only for point particles, it is possible to show mathematically that it also holds for the interaction between any pair of spherically symmetric mass distributions, provided that the position of the spherical masses are specified by their centers.

Gravitational Potential Energy

Gravity is Conservative

Gravitation is a conservative interaction, and, when internal to a system, should be represented by a potential energy rather than a force.

Form of the Potential Energy

It is customary to make the assignment that the gravitational potential energy of a system consisting of two objects goes to zero as the separation goes to infinity:

$ \lim_{r\rightarrow \infty} U^{G}(r) = 0$

With this assumption, we can determine the potential energy of the pair at any separation if we assume that Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation holds. The correct expression is:

$ U^{G}_{12} = - G \frac{m_{1}m_{2}}{r_{12}}$

Gravitational Potential Energy of a Many-Body System

In a system composed of many spherically symmetric objects, the total gravitational potential energy can be found by adding the contribution from each distinct interaction. Consider, for example, a system composed of four objects labeled 1, 2, 3 and 4. There are six distinct gravitational interactions among these bodies, each of which has an associated gravitational potential energy:

$ 1 \leftrightarrow 2 \mbox{ gives rise to }U_{12}$
$ 1 \leftrightarrow 3 \mbox{ gives rise to }U_{13}$
$ 1 \leftrightarrow 4 \mbox{ gives rise to }U_{14}$
$ 2 \leftrightarrow 3 \mbox{ gives rise to }U_{23}$
$ 2 \leftrightarrow 4 \mbox{ gives rise to }U_{24}$
$ 3 \leftrightarrow 4 \mbox{ gives rise to }U_{34}$

The total potential energy would then be given by:

$U_{sys} = U_{12} + U_{13} + U_{14} + U_{23} + U_{24} + U_{34}\;$

Note: It is important to beware of the tendency to double-count when computing the potential energy of a system. Although every interaction gives rise to two forces (e.g. F12 and F21) each interaction gives rise to only one potential energy. If U12 appears in the sum, then U21 must not appear (they are different names for the same energy).
